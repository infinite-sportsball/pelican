Indians Overcome Daulton's Two Homers With 15-Run Onslaught
The rain at Jacobs Field had settled into something steady by the time Al Leiter took the mound for the bottom of the third. He had been gifted a 4-0 lead by his team, with all four RBIs collected by his first baseman, Darren Daulton, who had a solo home run to right in the second inning on a 1-1 pitch, and another three-run shot to the same field in the top of the third. The Marlins in their dugout looked like men who had at last stumbled onto the version of this night they had been waiting for.

It lasted seven batters. Marquis Grissom, the Cleveland center fielder, singled up the middle on a 2-0 pitch. Bip Roberts followed with a soft infield hit. David Justice, the Cleveland designated hitter, spoiled two full-count pitches before dribbling a slow roller through the right side to load the bases. Sandy Alomar Jr, the Cleveland catcher, bounced into what should have been an inning-ending fielder's choice at the plate - Roberts thrown out sliding home - and Leiter, with two outs and the bases still loaded, walked Jim Thome on a 3-1 pitch, forcing Omar Vizquel across for the first Cleveland run. Manny Ramirez, the Indians' twenty-five-year-old right fielder, made sure of the rest a pitch later, driving a 1-0 line drive into the right-center gap at a hundred and one off the bat that cleared the bases and tied the game at four. Leiter walked off with nine hits already against his line and would not come out for the fourth - three and a third innings, eight runs, the shortest and worst start any Marlin has thrown across these sixty-four tellings.

What followed was less a comeback than an inventory. Justice doubled home two more in the fourth off Felix Heredia, and Alomar singled home Roberts behind him to make it 8-4. Vizquel scored Tony Fernandez in the fifth. Ramirez doubled home Alomar in the sixth. And in the seventh, against Jay Powell and then Dennis Cook, Cleveland put up five more on four hits and three walks - a slow, methodical demolition that included a bases-loaded walk to Fernandez, a fielder's-choice run by Matt Williams, and a run-scoring Grissom single that made it 15-5. Nineteen hits in all. Charles Nagy, the Cleveland starter, who had surrendered both Daulton homers and briefly looked like the losing pitcher in this particular telling, was long off the hook by then; Alvin Morman closed with two and two-thirds hitless innings and five strikeouts, the ball popping into Alomar's mitt in a rhythm the home crowd had begun to enjoy without quite trusting.

Renteria's grounder to first ended it, and Alomar - three hits, four runs batted in, the ball from Morman's last pitch still in his mitt - pulled off his mask behind the plate and walked out to meet his pitcher with a handshake and a grin. Behind him the roar of Jacobs Field cut through the cold rain, one game closer to the trophy this city had not held since 1948.
