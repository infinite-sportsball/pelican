Benches Clear After Hit Batter; Indians Answer With Eight-Single Barrage
Craig Counsell was the twenty-seven-year-old Marlins utility infielder with the strangest stance in baseball - bat cocked up over his head, standing on his toes like a man reaching for something on a high shelf. Four nights from now, against these same Indians, he would tie Game 7 of the World Series with a sacrifice fly in the ninth and score the winning run in the eleventh, and Cleveland would go home for the winter with his name lodged in its throat. But that was four nights away and this was tonight, and tonight, five days before he would break Cleveland's heart in Game 7, he was going to break the Mr. Nice Guy image, once and for all. Craig Counsell was going to charge the mound.

The score was 5-4 Cleveland in the top of the seventh. Charles Nagy had just given up a 333-foot Devon White home run down the right-field line to cut a three-run lead to one, and when Counsell stepped in behind White the first pitch came in at the ribs - not high, not at the head, but hard and unmistakably on purpose. The crowd noise did something Cleveland crowd noise almost never does in the middle of a World Series game: it caught in forty-four thousand throats at once, a collective gasp of the kind you hear when somebody kicks a golden retriever in public. It was Counsell. They had spent three games booing Sheffield and Bonilla and Alou, and the one Marlin they wouldn't boo was lying in the dirt at home plate. Counsell was slow to gather himself. Nagy, up on the mound, was already shouting something at him - arms out, chin up, the body language of a man who wanted everyone in the ballpark to know he wasn't sorry - and every head in the Florida dugout turned at the same instant. Counsell got up and limped toward first. He said something as he passed the mound. Nagy said something back. Whatever the third thing was, nobody heard it and nobody needed to, because Counsell dropped his bat and turned, and the entire Florida bench was already coming - they had been moving before he was, and the Marlins' bullpen door had banged open in right-center a full second before that. Gray home whites and white road grays met at the mound in a single knot forty men deep, a hockey line brawl in cleats with four separate scrums peeling off the main pile as it grew. Alomar had Nagy under one arm. Bobby Bonilla had someone from Cleveland by the jersey. Somebody's cap was in center field. The delay ran fifteen minutes. When it was over, Nagy and Counsell were walking to opposite tunnels tossed for the night, the umpires had thrown out one more from each side for good measure, and Jacobs Field was in a state of noise that had nothing to do with baseball anymore. It was the first and only ejection Craig Counsell would ever receive in his playing career, and he took it in Game 3 of a World Series, on the one October night of his life when a professional least wants to be walking off the field early.

What Cleveland did next was, in its own quiet way, the meaner thing. Paul Assenmacher got Renteria on a 6-4-3 to finish the top of the seventh. And then, off Jay Powell and Dennis Cook and Tony Saunders in succession, the Indians hit eight consecutive singles. Fernandez lined one to right. Matt Williams lined one to right. Grissom fouled off three straight full-count pitches and beat out an infield chopper. Vizquel singled and Fernandez scored. Roberts singled and Williams scored. Justice singled and Grissom scored on a throw home that arrived late. Alomar singled and Roberts scored on a throw home that arrived late. Thome, on 3-2, singled through the right side and Justice scored. Six runs, eight singles, no ball hit especially hard - the kind of rally that in a normal game looks lucky, and in this one looked like the power play that follows a five-minute major. The scoreboard read 11-4. Nobody in a Florida uniform argued a strike call for the rest of the night.

Counsell watched it from the visitors' clubhouse on a small television, in the uniform he had come to work in and would not now finish the night in. His teammates lost 11-4 in a game they had been one hit from tying, and lost it in the ugliest way a baseball team can lose one - by watching the other side collect eight consecutive base hits after a benches-clearing brawl their own quiet man had started. In this forty-ninth telling of the same October Tuesday, Craig Counsell had gotten his shot at the Indians five nights early, and it had cost his team the game.
