# Universe 56: Don Larsen is a Bum — Page Planning

## Context

Universe 56 simulates Game 5 of the 1956 World Series — Brooklyn Dodgers vs. New York Yankees — 51 times using Out
of the Park Baseball 20. In our universe, Don Larsen threw the only perfect game in World Series history on this
date. In Universe 56, the Dodgers win all 51 simulations. The average score is approximately Dodgers 7.7, Yankees
2.9. The project title is "Don Larsen is a Bum."

## Section 2: A Perfect Hand, A Losing Card

### Premise

Games where Don Larsen pitched well — sometimes brilliantly — and still lost. These are the timelines where Larsen
showed up, matched Maglie pitch for pitch, went deep into games, posted respectable game scores, and lost anyway
because the 1956 Brooklyn Dodgers were simply better. The perfect game wasn't Larsen playing above himself. It was
something else entirely — a glitch, a miracle, a thing that happened once and could never happen again.

- T17 is the most dramatic: Maglie balks with the bases loaded in the 9th. The Yankees claw within one run. Larsen himself bats with two outs, the tying run in scoring position, count 2-2. Called third strike. Game over. 159 pitches for nothing.

- What the list says about the perfect game: even the best versions of Larsen lose. The perfect game wasn't a great pitcher having a great day. It was something outside the normal probability space — an event so unlikely the simulation can't reproduce anything close to it in 51 attempts.

## Section 3: Bob Cerv: The Most Beautiful Train Wreck

### Premise

Bob Cerv was a corner outfielder for the 1956 New York Yankees. He was not a pitcher. In a significant number of
these timelines, he is pressed into duty as an emergency reliever when the Yankee bullpen runs dry. What follows is
never good. What follows is sometimes historically, cosmically, absurdly bad. He is the recurring character in a
horror franchise — you know what's coming, you can't stop watching.

### Aggregate Cerv Stats (estimated across appearances)

- Strikeouts: approximately 0 in most appearances

### Themes to Explore

- Cerv is an outfielder pitching in the World Series. That sentence alone is a short story.
- The recurring pattern: Cerv enters in the late innings, the game immediately descends into chaos. Walks, balks, wild pitches, inherited runners scoring — the full emergency.
- T24 is the masterpiece train wreck: Yankees lead 6-3 entering the 10th inning. Cerv's one job is to get three outs. Instead: walk, wild pitch, single, HBP, walk (bases loaded), balk, error, double. Five runs score. 43 pitches, 23 strikes. The Bronx is "howling for Cerv's head."
- T08: 54 pitches in one inning. Twelve batters faced. Five walks. He throws more balls than strikes. His ERA after one inning: 54.00. Imagine if Cerv had pitched a full game: it wouldn't be a football score, it would be a basketball score.
- T11: 63.00 ERA. He balks in a run. The recap describes the 9th inning as Brooklyn "no longer playing baseball so much as conducting an audit."
- The philosophical dimension: Cerv is what happens when the simulation breaks the Yankees so thoroughly they have

  contest and becomes a performance of surrender.

## Section 5: The Sad, Short Life of a Yankee Lead

### Premise

Games where the Yankees held a lead — sometimes late, sometimes comfortable — and lost anyway. 

This is the rivalry page. These are the timelines where 64,519 people at Yankee Stadium stood up and believed.
Where Mantle hit a home run and the crowd noise had that particular warmth of a building that thinks it has just
seen the tone of a game established. Where Larsen himself singled in a run and the ghosts of previous Octobers
leaned forward.

In every single case, it was a lie. Brooklyn took it back — not just the lead, but the belief
that the lead had meant something. In 51 timelines, no Yankee lead is safe. No Yankee lead is real. The scoreboard
is playing a practical joke on 64,519 people, and Brooklyn is in on it every time.

## Section 6: The Barber's 51 Shaves

### Premise

Sal Maglie's nickname was "The Barber." He shaved the corners of the plate and threw inside to opposing hitters
close enough to make them flinch. In our universe, nobody remembers what Sal Maglie did on On October 8, 1956. He
is a footnote to Larsen's miracle — the losing pitcher in the only perfect game in World Series history. In
Universe 56, Maglie is the most dominant pitcher in any World Series, real or simulated, that has ever been played.
He pitches 9.0 or more innings in 47 of 51 games. He throws complete games in at least 46 of them. He wins at least 49.
Fifty one times in a row, a thirty nine year old Sal Maglie repeats this feat, on the same Monday afternoon, in the
same enemy ballpark, against the same lineup. 

### The Range of Maglie

| Category | Timelines | Details |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Complete-game shutouts | T04 (6-0), T06 (2-0), T15 (4-0), T21 (12-0), T23 (1-0), T27 (2-0), T28 (7-0), T34 (13-0) | 8 shutouts. The real Larsen threw one perfect game. Maglie threw 8 shutouts. |
| CG, 3 hits or fewer | T23 (3 H, 105 pitches), T29 (3 H, 108 pitches), T09 (3 H), T05 (3 H), T34 (3 H), T46 (3 H), T38 (3 H) | At least 7 games where the entire Yankee lineup — Mantle, Berra, Slaughter, Martin — gets 3 hits or fewer. |
| CG, 10+ hits allowed | T01 (12 H), T10 (10 H), T16 (10 H), T22 (14 H), T25 (11 H), T40 (10 H), T41 (10 H) | He wins these too. 14 hits allowed in T22 — still a complete game, still a win. |
| 130+ pitches | T01 (145), T17 (137), T27 (139), T16 (151) | At minimum 4 games where Maglie throws 130+ pitches and finishes the game. |
| Sub-9 IP (pulled or left early) | T02 (8.2), T08 (7.2), T41 (8.0), T45 (7.0) | Only 4 games in 51 where Maglie doesn't finish. In T08 (26-15), even Maglie had limits. |
| Extra innings | T24 (9.2 IP) | Goes into the 10th. Still wins. |

### Key Performances

- **T23: The Masterpiece** — 9 IP, 3 H, 0 R, 6 K, 1 BB, 105 pitches. Maglie throws a 3-hit shutout while Larsen throws a gem of his own (9 IP, 5 H, 1 ER, game score 75). Maglie wins 1-0 on a Campanella homer. Strikes out Berra swinging with the tying run at first, 2 outs, 9th inning.
- **T17: Survival** — 9 IP, 137 pitches. Balks with bases loaded in the 9th, giving the Yankees life. Strikes out McDougald. Then Carey lines a 2-out double to make it 6-5. With the tying run at second, Larsen bats — called strike three, bat on shoulder. "The ball had already landed in Campanella's glove before Larsen's brain understood the at-bat was over."
- **T22: The Endurance Test** — 9 IP, 14 hits allowed, a 42-minute rain delay, and a complete game in a 10-7 win that lasted 4 hours 43 minutes. He bent in every direction and broke in none.
- **T16: The Iron Man** — 9 IP, 151 pitches, 10 hits allowed. Wins 11-4. 151 pitches in a World Series game that he never leaves.
- **T08: The Only Mortal Afternoon** — 7.2 IP in the 26-15 bloodbath. Even Maglie gets roughed up. Still gets the win. His game score in T08 is around 23 — the worst of his career across all timelines. His best (T23) is 86. The range between his worst and best is 63 points, and even the worst is a win.

### Themes to Explore

- The historical injustice: In our universe, Maglie is remembered as the guy who *lost* the perfect game. In Universe 56, he's the greatest World Series pitcher who ever lived. History gave the afternoon to Larsen. The simulation gives it back.
- The nickname as metaphor: "The Barber" walks into the enemy's house — the House That Ruth Built — and gives the home team a close shave. Fifty-one times. At their own barber's chair.
- The physical absurdity: Maglie is 39 years old. He throws 9+ innings in 47 of 51 games. He throws 151 pitches in one of them. He is not a young man performing a feat of endurance. He is an old man who simply refuses to leave.
- The anti-Larsen: Larsen's perfect game was a miracle — an event so improbable the simulation can't get close in 51 tries. Maglie's dominance is the opposite: it's the *expected* outcome, the thing the simulation says should have happened, repeated with the steady inevitability of gravity.

## Section 7: You Don't Have To Go Home But You Can't Win Here

It rained on Yankee Stadium in 14 of 51 timelines. In those 14 games, the Dodgers went 14-0 with an average score of approximately 6.4 to 2.6 — beating the Yankees in their own house while the sky did the same from above. Three of those games featured rain delays: a 16-minute delay in the 8th inning of T05, a 42-minute delay in the 5th inning of T22, and a *seventy-minute* delay in the 3rd inning of T46. This page is about what Yankee Stadium looks like in the 7th, 8th, and 9th innings of a rain-soaked blowout loss — the home faithful heading for the exits, collars up, while the Brooklyn partisans who took the subway across the bridge stay in their seats, soaking wet and delirious, savoring every last run scored in a building that was never meant to belong to them.

### Themes to Explore

- The T05 recap is the thesis statement for the entire page, already written by the simulation: Yankee fans leaving, Brooklyn fans staying, the building belonging to the wrong borough.
- T19 is the crown jewel: a Snider grand slam in the 9th inning of a 12-2 game, in the rain, at 57 degrees, landing in empty seats. The people who were supposed to be sitting there have already left. The people who are still there — who came from Brooklyn, who took the train across the bridge to the Bronx — are the only ones watching. And they're loving it.
- T46's 70-minute rain delay in the 3rd inning: you are a Yankee fan. Your team is losing. It starts raining. You sit under the grandstand for over an hour. The game resumes. Your team loses 5-1. You have been at Yankee Stadium for nearly five hours. You are wet and your team has lost.
- T22 is the psychological warfare version: the Yankees actually came back from 5-0 down to lead 6-5. Then it rained for 42 minutes. During those 42 minutes, you believed. Then Brooklyn scored 5 in the 9th. Total game time: 4 hours 43 minutes.
- The rivalry as architecture: Yankee Stadium was built to intimidate visitors. It was the House That Ruth Built, the cathedral of the pinstripes, the biggest and loudest building in baseball. In the rain games, it becomes a trap — not for Brooklyn, but for the home fans, who paid for tickets and took the subway and now have to sit in the cold rain watching the borough across the bridge celebrate in their living room.
- The contrast with reality: on the real October 8, 1956, Yankee Stadium was the site of the greatest individual performance in baseball history. In these 14 rain timelines, it's a cold, wet theater where 64,519 people watch the visiting team average 6.4 runs while the rain falls on everyone equally but only the Brooklyn fans are smiling.
