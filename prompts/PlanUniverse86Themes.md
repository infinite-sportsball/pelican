# Universe 86 — Seven Lists

86 games. Same two starters every time. Boston brought a Hall of Fame infielder and a pitcher who would win 354. Houston brought a thirty-nine-year-old Nolan Ryan and asked him to do something nobody has ever asked a starting pitcher to do.

He said yes.

The numbers say:

- Nolan Ryan struck out **717 batters** in 663⅓ IP. Roger Clemens struck out 541. They faced the same lineup.
- Roger Clemens — at the plate — went 9-for-225 with 156 strikeouts. He hit .040.
- Houston walked off Boston in the home half **11 times**, three of them by 3-run rallies in the 9th from a tied or trailing position.
- Bill Buckner hit eight home runs. Six were off Nolan Ryan. The other two came in one game, in the same game, off the same reliever, in extra innings.
- Glenn Davis hit fourteen home runs. **Thirteen** were off Roger Clemens.

Seven lists.

---

## LIST 1 — Nine men, three innings, six pitches: a study in surrender

Strikeouts as the answer, every time. The premise: Boston's lineup hit .314 (Boggs), had Jim Rice in the cleanup spot, had Marty Barrett who almost never struck out. They were not bums. But against Nolan Ryan they were three specific outs.

1. **Game 66 — Ryan strikes out 15 in a complete game.** Boston gets 3 hits in 27 outs. Game time: 2:50. **Dave Henderson goes 0-for-4 with 4 K.** A man can strike out 4 times in a game. He cannot strike out 5. He has only four chances. Henderson took every one of them.
2. **Game 62 — Ryan strikes out 14 in a complete game.** 5 hits allowed. **Henderson again goes 0-for-4 with 4 K.** Two games, two months apart, identical surrender.
3. **Game 56 — Ryan throws 10 innings, strikes out 13, gives up 1 unearned run, loses 1-0.** Clemens goes 10 innings back. The line score: a column of 0s and a single 1 in Boston's tenth. **Both pitchers threw a 10-inning complete game on the same night.** Ryan's reward for the cleanest start of his series was an L.
4. **Game 47 — Ryan strikes out 13.** Boston wins 7-6 anyway. Bill Buckner — Bill *Buckner* — homers off him with two on in the 1st inning. The man Ryan faced 86 times took him out of the park ten different innings of ten different games and he had to keep coming back.
5. **Game 72 — Ryan 13 K in 7⅓.** Houston loses 2-1.
6. **Game 3 — 12 K. Boston wins 6-3.**
7. **Game 77 — 12 K. Boston wins 3-2.**
8. **Game 51 — 11 K, 5 walks, 1 hit allowed. Houston wins 2-1.** Ryan walked 5 men, surrendered exactly one base hit, and won. Boston had ONE hit in the whole game (M. Barrett, second inning). They scored that 1 run on that 1 hit and lost.
9. **Game 82 — 11 K, 0 earned runs in 7 IP. Houston loses 2-1.**

Ryan averaged **8.34 K per game** for 86 starts. Bob Gibson's career best season was 8.84. Ryan put up Bob-Gibson-best-season for three straight months. **Dave Henderson struck out 153 times in 295 at-bats, a 51.9% rate.** That is not a slump. That is a relationship. Ryan, somewhere around game 21, learned how to delete Dave Henderson, and never forgot the recipe.

## LIST 2 — Glenn Davis kept hitting Roger Clemens

A list of duels — built around the **batter-pitcher pair**. 105 home runs were hit in this series. **58 were given up by Roger Clemens.** The names that did it kept doing it. Same pitcher. Same swing. Same result.

1. **Glenn Davis vs. Roger Clemens. 13 home runs in 14 series HRs.** Games 11, 20, 23, 40, 46, 55, 58, 61, 64, 74, 79, 83, 84. The first was a 9th-inning leadoff walk-off in Game 11. The last was Game 84. Between them: 73 games. Davis homered off only one other pitcher all series — a leadoff walk-off in the 11th inning of Game 5, off Calvin Schiraldi. Davis's only known method of hitting a home run was Roger Clemens.
2. **Denny Walling vs. Roger Clemens. 14 of 15.** Every Walling home run but one came off Roger Clemens. Walling slashed .306 with 15 HR — fourteen of those long balls were Clemens fastballs.
3. **Kevin Bass vs. Roger Clemens. 10 of 10.** Kevin Bass hit zero home runs against any other pitcher. Across 86 games against four different Boston relievers, with 320 at-bats, Kevin Bass's home-run output against pitchers not named Roger Clemens was: zero.
4. **Bill Buckner vs. Nolan Ryan. 6 of 8 HR.** The other two came in one game, in extra innings, in Game 47. Buckner went deep on Ryan in games 6, 16, 34, 43, 63, 76. He hit a 3-run homer in the 8th inning of game 34. He hit a leadoff homer in the 1st inning of game 16. The shape of his bat against Ryan was not a slump. It was a personal grievance.
5. **Wade Boggs vs. Nolan Ryan. 4 of 4.** Wade Boggs is a future Hall of Famer who hit 24 HR in real-life 1986. In this series he hit four. All against the same man.
6. **Marty Barrett vs. Nolan Ryan. 3 of 3.** All Barrett's HRs came against Ryan.
7. **Jim Rice vs. Nolan Ryan. 4 of 4.** All Rice's HRs.
8. **Dwight Evans vs. Nolan Ryan. 6 of 8 HR.**
9. **Dave Henderson vs. Nolan Ryan. 4 of 5.** The same Dave Henderson who struck out 153 times. Yes, that one. He homered off Ryan four separate times. Ryan does not have a comfortable feeling about Dave Henderson. He has a *large* feeling, with two ends.
10. **Roger Clemens vs. Nolan Ryan. 1 of 1.** Game 2, fifth inning, no one on, no one out. Roger Clemens hit a home run off Nolan Ryan. He took 224 more at-bats and never managed it again. He hit a home run the second time he ever batted against this man and never again. **Clemens batted 225 times. He struck out 156 times. The other 69 times, he got 9 hits and one of them was a home run off the second-best pitcher of all time.**

The thing that makes this a list and not a coincidence: against Houston relievers, Boston batters hit a total of **3 home runs all series.** Three. Eighty-six games, two relievers seeing extensive work, three home runs. Boston could only homer off Ryan. Houston could only homer off Clemens. The starters absorbed nearly every long ball in the series. There are entire full seasons of major-league baseball where this is not true.

## LIST 3 — The Boston shutout, ranked by how rude it was

A list ranked by a **custom statistic** — Boston's offensive humiliation — defined as: **(Nolan Ryan's strikeouts) ÷ (game length in hours).** We are looking for: how viciously and how *quickly* Boston was put away.

In 86 games, Boston was shut out **TEN TIMES.** Houston was shut out 4. The Red Sox, with Wade Boggs and Jim Rice and Dwight Evans, scored zero runs ten different times. The shape of how fast it happened matters.

10. **Game 68 — HOU 4-0.** Ryan 8⅓ IP, 8 K, in 3:00. Rate: 2.67. Average rudeness.
9. **Game 8 — HOU 3-0.** Ryan complete game, 9 K, 3:14. Rate: 2.78.
8. **Game 57 — HOU 3-0.** Ryan 8⅓, 10 K, **2:52.** Rate: 3.49.
7. **Game 37 — HOU 5-0.** Ryan 8⅓, 6 K. Clemens lasted 6.
6. **Game 83 — HOU 7-0.** Ryan complete game, 10 K, **143 pitches.** He worked. He won by margin.
5. **Game 79 — HOU 5-0.** Ryan complete game, 8 K, **94 pitches in 2:38.** The fastest game in the series. Rate: 3.04. Ryan threw 94 pitches, shut out the Red Sox, and was home before the late local news. Cruel because it was efficient.
4. **Game 66 — HOU 5-0.** Ryan complete game, **15 K**, 3 hits, **2:50.** Rate: **5.29.** This is the game that breaks the scale. The single best pitching performance in the dataset, thrown in a blistering 170 minutes.
3. **Game 62 — HOU 3-0.** Ryan complete game, **14 K**, 5 hits, 3:12. Rate: 4.38.
2. **Game 45 — HOU 1-0.** Ryan complete game, 7 K, **3 hits, 103 pitches.** Boston scratched and clawed and got nothing. Houston also got barely anything: their one run was Ryan's only break. The smallest possible margin in baseball, achieved without violating any axioms.
1. **Game 44 — HOU 2-0.** Ryan went 6⅓ and *did not finish the game.* He **walked seven Red Sox.** Boston still got shut out. 3 hits, 7 free passes, zero runs. **The Red Sox were handed seven first bases and forgot what to do.** This is the game that wins the list, because it answers a question nobody thought to ask: *Could you actually shut out the 1986 Red Sox by walking everybody?* Yes. You can.

The deep cut: across all 86 games, Boston *won* on a night where they stranded **27 runners** in a single 9-inning game (Game 32 — final 4-2). A team that strands 27 men should lose 30-2. They won. **In thirteen separate games, Boston stranded 25 or more.** The Red Sox in this series have a particular relationship with second base. They reach it. They look at it. They walk back to the dugout.

Cumulative individual LOB: BOS 1,410 vs. HOU 1,120. The Red Sox left almost three hundred more runners on base than the Astros did.

They got there. They just couldn't figure out what to do once they arrived.

## LIST 4 — Low-pitch-count masterpieces

A Maddux is a complete-game shutout thrown in fewer than 100 pitches. Greg Maddux did it 13 times in a 23-year career. It is the form of pitching dominance that announcers will literally interrupt a broadcast to mention, because it is the rarest possible expression of one man telling nine other men to sit down. No fastball-only pitcher has any business throwing one. No power pitcher should be able to do it. A Maddux requires that every inning end on three pitches or four, that nobody walks, that nobody chases, that no swinging strikeouts are allowed because they all eat eight pitches.

In 172 World Series starts in this duel — 86 Clemens, 86 Ryan, between two of the greatest pitchers of their generation in a series that wouldn't quit — exactly one Maddux was thrown.

### 1. Game 79 — Nolan Ryan, the only Maddux of the series. **94 pitches, 9 innings.**

9.0 IP. 6 H. 1 BB. 8 K. **0 R.** A 5-0 Houston win in **2 hours, 38 minutes** — also the fastest game of the series, by nine minutes.

Stop and read those numbers again. **Nolan Ryan.** The man whose entire reputation is the triple-digit fastball that nobody can hit but also nobody can catch. *That* Nolan Ryan threw 94 pitches in a complete game, walked one guy, struck out eight, and pitched a shutout. **He pitched a Maddux.** Ryan, of all pitchers, decided to be Greg Maddux for one night, in a World Series pitching duel against Roger Clemens, against the Boston Red Sox.

The Red Sox got six hits. They could not move a single one of them across the plate. Ryan averaged 10.4 pitches per inning. The game was over before 11:00 PM. It is the cleanest, leanest, most economical expression of pitching dominance in the dataset, and it was thrown by the man you would least expect to throw it.

### 2. Game 45 — Nolan Ryan, near-Maddux. **103 pitches, 9 innings.**

9.0 IP. 3 H. 3 BB. 7 K. **0 R.** A **1-0** Houston win. Three pitches over the Maddux line. Houston scored once, late. Ryan never gave anything back. A tight, almost-quiet 103.

### 3. Game 71 — Roger Clemens, 9 IP, **2 hits**, 0 R, 118 pitches.

The **lowest-hit complete game in the dataset.** Boston won 3-0. Clemens allowed two base hits in 27 outs and walked one. This is the canonical Clemens game — heat, swing-and-miss, work for every out, no surrender — and it is the cleanest expression of his stuff in the series. He gave up two hits. He still needed twenty-four more pitches than Ryan needed to give up six.

### 4. Game 13 — Roger Clemens, 9 IP, 0 R, 113 pitches.

A 1-0 Boston win. Boston scored their one run on three hits across the entire night. Clemens went the distance, six harmless hits, two walks. The most efficient complete-game shutout Clemens managed all series — still 19 pitches over the Maddux line.

### 5. Game 38 — Roger Clemens, 9 IP, 0 R, 115 pitches.

A 6-0 Boston win — the rare night the offense showed up. Clemens went the distance and threw a clinic.

### 6. Game 8 — Nolan Ryan, 9 IP, 0 R, **5 BB**, 116 pitches.

Ryan walked five Red Sox in this complete-game shutout and nobody scored. The Maddux line says you can't walk people. Ryan said *watch me.*

The deep cut: Clemens authored two non-shutout efficiency masterpieces too — **Game 78** (8 IP, 92 pitches, 6 K, 1 ER, won 2-1) and **Game 77** (8 IP, 92 pitches, 4 K, 1 ER, won 3-2). Back-to-back starts. Both 92 pitches. Both 8 innings. Both wins by a single run. He left those games with the Maddux line still in reach — 8 more pitches to go nine — and the bullpen finished it. Clemens threw four complete-game shutouts and his most efficient one was thirteen pitches over the line. Ryan threw six and broke 105 twice.

The cleanest game ever pitched in this series was Game 79 by Nolan Ryan, on a night when the World Series stopped to watch one man stop being the Ryan Express and be Greg Maddux for two and a half hours instead.

## LIST 5 — Some kinda vacation, huh pal?

Picture the guy. Forty-something. Boston accent. Real Red Sox fan, the kind whose father raised him on Yastrzemski stories. He flew his family down to Houston for Game 2 of the World Series. Stretched the budget. Got the kids out of school. Mortgaged the basement to afford four seats in the upper deck of the Astrodome on a Wednesday night in October.

He saw a 4-3 walk-off Astros win in the bottom of the ninth.

Then he saw it again.

Then he saw it again. Then again. Then again.

Then he tried to leave.

He could not leave.

In a normal big-league season, a contender will host four or five walk-off wins all year. Houston walked off Boston **eleven times** in 86 games — a **12.8%** walk-off rate, more than triple the historical norm. **For the Astros fan with season tickets, it was the greatest stretch of home baseball ever played.** Every other Tuesday the place erupted. Glenn Davis. Jose Cruz. Craig Reynolds. The bottom of the ninth, two runs down, three runs gone, ballgame over, hat into the air.

The Boston traveler who landed at Hobby on October 18 with a return flight on the 25th was, depending on how you measure, the unluckiest person ever born or the luckiest — he had front-row tickets to one of the great offensive late-inning shows in baseball history. The fact that his team kept losing them did not seem to factor into whether the games kept happening. The games kept happening. The games will keep happening.

### The eleven walk-offs

1. **Game 2 (B9) — down 3-1, scored 3, won 4-3.** Identical disaster #1.
2. **Game 22 (B9) — down 2-0, scored 3, won 3-2.** Identical disaster #2. Boston had been shut out for eight innings and finally scored two in the top of the 9th. The lead lasted ten minutes.
3. **Game 29 (B9) — down 4-2, scored 3, won 5-4.** Identical disaster #3.
4. **Game 5 (B11)** — Davis leadoff bomb. The crowd had stayed for extra innings and got rewarded on the first pitch of the bottom of the 11th.
5. **Game 11 (B9)** — Davis leadoff bomb off Clemens. Down 1-0. Two-run shot. Game over.
6. **Game 16 (B12)** — twelve innings. One run. **The Boston tourist sat through twelve innings, paid Astrodome prices for a Coke and a hot dog, watched his team lose 4-3 at midnight.**
7. **Game 17 (B11)** — eleven innings. One run. Final: HOU 5, BOS 4.
8. **Game 18 (B9)** — Cruz 2-run walk-off HR off Stanley.
9. **Game 19 (B9)** — tied 3-3, scored 1. The crowd's fifth walk-off in nine days.
10. **Game 53 (B9) — Reynolds, two-out walk-off HR off Stanley.** The last possible swing. Tied 2-2, two outs, nobody on. Reynolds connects. The Astrodome roof comes off (figuratively; the roof doesn't open).
11. **Game 73 (B9)** — down 7-6, scored 2, won 8-7. The slugfest finale, the 15-run blowout, ends on the home plate.

### The three identical disasters at the heart of it

The shape of three of those wins is the part that should make you sit up. **Down exactly two entering the bottom of the ninth. Scored exactly three. Won by exactly one.** Three times. Game 2. Game 22. Game 29. Same plot, same beat, same final brushstroke. Across 86 games, Houston entered the bottom of the 9th with a chance to erase a two-run deficit by exactly three runs, and they did it three times in three different ways without once doing it incorrectly.

The Astros fan in section 309 in seat 12 has, by this point, learned the rhythm. The Boston tourist has learned a different rhythm. He's learning it on the trip home, which is taking longer than expected, because the highway only loops back to the parking lot.

### The HOU 8th-inning supernova — why the Houston fan keeps going home happy

The Astros didn't just walk off. They woke up *late.* Across 86 games:

| Inning | BOS runs | HOU runs |
|--------|----------|----------|
| 6 | 14 | **36** |
| 7 | 31 | 27 |
| 8 | 26 | **41** |

Houston's most productive inning was the 8th. It wasn't even close. **Houston outscored Boston 77-40 in the 6th and 8th combined.** Once the bullpen door swung open and Bob Stanley or Calvin Schiraldi started warming up, the Astrodome turned into a kitchen. Boston's late-game bats slowed; Houston's woke up. That pattern held for 86 straight games without breaking once.

For the Astros fan with season tickets, this is paradise. You arrive at 7:00, you have a beer, you watch six quiet innings, and at some point in the seventh or eighth your team starts a rally and finishes it. **Every night.** For three months. The greatest run of late-inning home-team theater in the history of organized baseball.

For the Boston tourist, it's the same thing in reverse, and he can't get back to Logan. The taxi he called only goes to the Astrodome. The flight he booked only flies to Houston. He files a complaint at the front desk of the Hyatt. The night manager smiles politely and explains that the lobby only has one exit, and it leads to the ballpark.

He puts the credit card down for a fourth night and asks for a wake-up call. The wake-up call comes at the same time it came yesterday. He goes to the same game tonight. The Astros score 3 in the 8th. Final score: HOU 4, BOS 3.

Some kinda vacation, huh pal?

## LIST 6 — The eight-game stretch where Nolan Ryan ate the World Series

From game 15 through game 22, Houston won eight in a row. Eight games. Eight wins. **Nolan Ryan was named Player of the Game in seven of them.**

The exception was Game 20, in which Glenn Davis hit two home runs, and Houston won 5-4 anyway, and Ryan still threw 6 innings. Even on his off night, Ryan's worst game in the streak was a quality start.

The list of POGs in those eight games:
- **Game 15** — Nolan Ryan
- **Game 16** — Nolan Ryan (12-inning walk-off win)
- **Game 17** — Nolan Ryan (11-inning walk-off win)
- **Game 18** — Nolan Ryan (9-inning walk-off win)
- **Game 19** — Nolan Ryan (9-inning walk-off win)
- **Game 20** — Glenn Davis (2 HR)
- **Game 21** — Nolan Ryan
- **Game 22** — Nolan Ryan (the 0-2 → 3-2 disaster)

Five walk-offs in eight games. Ryan POG in seven. **For two straight weeks, Nolan Ryan was the entire National League.** He delivered 8 innings of one-or-two-run ball every fourth night and the Astrodome crowd took the rest of the game home with them.

The streak's bookends are the most poetic part. Game 15: Ryan goes 8 IP, 4 H, 1 R, 6 K, gets the win 3-1. Game 22: Ryan goes 8⅓ IP, 5 H, 2 R (0 ER), 10 K, gets a no-decision but is named POG of a game his bullpen tied and his lineup walked off in the 9th. **Eight consecutive starts in which one man's two-week stretch of pitching tilted the entire World Series.**

Across this 86-game series, Ryan was named Player of the Game **46 times.** That's 53.5% of all games, in a series featuring nine other Astros and a Hall of Fame Boston pitcher and a Hall of Fame Boston third baseman. **More than half the time.** And in the 8-game streak from g15 to g22, the rate jumped to 87.5%.

This is what it looks like when one starting pitcher's stuff takes over a World Series. There are no comparable streaks in real baseball history because no real pitcher has ever started 86 games in three months against the same lineup. There is no point of reference. There is only the data, which says: **for two weeks in the middle of an endless World Series, Nolan Ryan owned every Wednesday.**

## LIST 7 — The Rocket, the labrum, and the man who showed up to throw 86 games

### Where Roger Clemens actually was, on October 19, 1986

To understand what the 86-game version of Roger Clemens means, you have to know the man the simulator picked up off his real-life mound and dropped into a loop.

He was **24 years old.** His third major-league season. His **first fully healthy one.**

In August of 1984 — his rookie year — his shoulder started barking. By 1985 it was finished. **A torn labrum.** *Dr. James Andrews* opened him up and cleaned it out arthroscopically (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Clemens). At the time of the surgery, the procedure was rare enough on a starting pitcher that the medical guidance was, essentially, *we don't know if you'll come back.* Pitchers didn't come back from labrum tears. Then Clemens did.

He came back as a *separate human being.* On April 29, 1986, at Fenway against Seattle, he became **the first pitcher in major-league history to strike out 20 batters in a nine-inning game** (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Clemens). He started the All-Star Game. He won it. He won the All-Star MVP. He went **24-4 with a 2.48 ERA, 238 K, 254 IP** (baseball-reference.com/players/c/clemero02.shtml). He won the **AL Cy Young.** He won the **AL MVP.** No starting pitcher had won the MVP since Vida Blue in 1971; the next one wouldn't be until Verlander in 2011 (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Clemens). The 1986 Roger Clemens is one of the great single-season comebacks in baseball history.

Then Boston went to the ALCS. The Angels jumped to a 3-1 series lead. Clemens started **three games in eight days** — Game 1 (Oct 7), Game 4 (Oct 11), Game 7 (Oct 15) (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1986_American_League_Championship_Series). He took the loss in Game 1 — Ruppert Jones, Wally Joyner, and Brian Downing put up four runs on him in the second. He went into the 9th of Game 4 with a 3-1 lead, was lifted two outs short of a complete game, and watched the bullpen lose it in eleven. He won Game 7. **22⅔ innings, 4.37 ERA, 1-1, on three days' rest each time** (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1986_American_League_Championship_Series).

Game 1 of the World Series was October 18 at Shea. Clemens was scheduled for Game 2 the next night. **Three days' rest. Again.** A 24-year-old, fifteen months out of shoulder surgery, who had thrown 254 regular-season innings and 22⅔ more in the most intense ALCS of the 1980s.

He started Game 2 against Dwight Gooden. He had a 6-2 lead in the bottom of the 5th. **He was pulled after facing three batters that inning, leaving runners on 1st and 3rd, with no decision** (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1986_World_Series). Roughly **4⅓ innings.** Steve Crawford got the W. The blister story — the famous one — wasn't this game. *That* was Game 6, where he was lifted in the 8th and McNamara and Clemens later argued in public over whose idea it had been (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Clemens). But the *seed* of the blister, the worn-down hand and the worn-down arm, was already there in October. He had thrown a lot of pitches. He was tired. He was twenty-four.

That's the Roger Clemens the simulator had on file. A pitcher who was, in real life, a partially-rebuilt shoulder, a Cy Young / MVP regular season, a 22⅔-inning ALCS, and a Game 2 that ended early. A guy whose arm had been managed carefully for two years and who, in the actual record, did not finish either of his two World Series starts.

The simulator gave him **the ball every night for 86 nights.**

What he did with it, the data already knows.

### The streak that defines him: thirteen straight 8-inning starts

From **Game 18 through Game 30** — thirteen consecutive starts — Roger Clemens pitched **at least 8 innings every single time.** Eight, eight, eight, eight, eight. Thirteen times in a row. Across those thirteen games he averaged 8.05 IP per outing and threw 1,558 pitches — that's 119 pitches per start, every fifth night, except in this universe it was every night, and he never blinked.

In real-life MLB, no pitcher has thrown 13 consecutive 8-inning starts since the 1970s. Clemens did it on a Tuesday. Then again on a Wednesday. Then again. He went 5-7 in the streak. **The losses were not on him.** He gave up 3 ER or fewer in nine of the thirteen. The Boston offense scored 2 runs or fewer in seven of the thirteen.

Then he did it again. From g38 through g44 — **seven more straight 8-IP starts.** Then again. From g54 through g60 — **another seven.** Then again. From g67 through g72 — **another six.** **Roger Clemens threw at least 8 innings in 33 of his 86 starts — 38% of the time — and on four separate stretches he stacked at least six in a row.**

### The streak nobody talks about: he never lost a game until Game 8

Clemens's first seven starts: **no losses.** Three wins, four no-decisions. Through the first 56 innings of his series he had a 2.57 ERA, struck out 39, and watched his bullpen blow two leads (g1 and g2). He started the entire World Series **3-0** before he took an L. The series didn't humble him until game 8, which he lost 3-0 — to a Ryan complete-game shutout.

### The 4-start no-HR streak

Clemens gave up **58 home runs** in the series. He gave up zero in **games 13, 14, 15, and 16** — four straight starts. He went 2-1 with a no-decision in those four games, threw 34 innings, and surrendered no contact that ever left the yard. **It was the longest stretch of clean ball he had all series.**

### The seven complete games

Seven complete games. Four shutouts. **Game 71** was a two-hit complete-game shutout — the lowest hit total he allowed in any game all series, the cleanest expression of his stuff. **Game 56** was the 10-inning 1-0 masterpiece, the only 10-inning complete-game victory in the dataset. **Game 38** was a 6-0 four-hit gem. **Game 13** was a 1-0 six-hit complete game in which Boston also got just three hits and won 1-0. Across his seven CGs he was 7-0 with a 0.71 ERA. **When Roger Clemens decided he was going to finish a baseball game, the result was never in doubt.**

### The hot streak: g54 → g56, three Players of the Game in a row

His longest POG streak: **three straight.** Game 54 (8 IP, 1 ER, won 4-2). Game 55 (8 IP, 3 ER, won 5-3). **Game 56 (10 IP, 0 R, won 1-0 in extra innings).** Three consecutive starts, three POG awards, and a 10-inning 1-0 complete game capping the run. That stretch is the closest thing this series has to a Cy Young moment for Clemens. Three starts. 26 innings. 4 ER. **0.69 ERA across the streak.**

### The streak that broke him: six straight losses to end the series

From **Game 79 through Game 84** — six consecutive Clemens losses. The longest L-streak in the dataset by either pitcher. He gave up 5+ ER in three of them. Game 81 was a 12-hit, 6-ER nightmare. Game 83 he lasted 6⅓ and gave up 11 hits. By the end he was on fumes — 675⅓ innings into a season that wouldn't quit, a guy whose arm had been throwing 119 pitches every fourth night for three months. **He still made the start every time.** He still went 8 innings in five of those six losses. His arm was finished. His turn in the rotation was not.

### The number behind the W-L: 28 Players of the Game

For all the bad-luck no-decisions and the 39 losses, Clemens was named POG **28 times.** Houston ran on Ryan; Boston ran on Clemens. When the offense showed up, Clemens won. When it didn't, he kept pitching anyway. **3.07 ERA. 541 strikeouts. 7 complete games. 4 shutouts. 33 starts of 8+ IP.** A career year by any normal measurement, played out 86 times against the same nine guys, three of whom kept hitting his fastball over fences.

### The real-life coda

Read the streaks above against the real-life dossier. The flesh-and-blood Roger Clemens of October 1986 was a 24-year-old fifteen months out of shoulder surgery whose Game 2 World Series start lasted **4⅓ innings** before he was lifted with a six-run lead. The Roger Clemens the simulator built out of his data threw **675⅓ innings** of 3.07 ERA baseball across 86 starts in three months, including thirteen straight 8-IP starts and a 10-inning 1-0 complete game on a night when his arm should have been in a sling. The labrum the real Clemens had repaired in 1985 was, in this universe, asked to go to the mound on three days' rest, eighty-five times in a row, against the same lineup, behind a defense that committed errors and a closer who gave up walk-offs.

It said yes every time.

The simulator does not know that Roger Clemens almost didn't have a career. It does not know about Dr. James Andrews. It does not know that the man Boston was leaning on every night was, fifteen months earlier, a question mark. It knows only the shape of the data — the 24-4, the 2.48, the 238 — and it gave him 86 chances to be that pitcher. He took every single one. **The Rocket, in a universe that asked him to keep being The Rocket forever, did.**
