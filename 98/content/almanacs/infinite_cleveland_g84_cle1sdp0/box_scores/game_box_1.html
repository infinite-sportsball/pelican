Indians Defeat Padres
Bartolo Colon, the Cleveland starter, spent three brisk October hours making the San Diego lineup look like people trying to pick a lock with a fork — seven innings, six hits, no runs, six strikeouts on a hundred pitches — and when he walked off the mound after the seventh, the Jacobs Field crowd rose for him in the loose, grateful way crowds rise for a starter who has held a one-run lead longer than seemed fair. The night was forty-one degrees and clear, the cold visible in the way the ball came out of both starters' hands, in the way every ground ball seemed to find leather. This was the eighty-fourth telling of the same Tuesday, and somewhere under the noise was the unsettling sense that all of it had happened before.

The lone run came in the bottom of the second, off the Padres' ace Kevin Brown. Brian Giles, the Indians' DH, opened by skipping a 2-2 grounder down the first-base line for a double, and Enrique Wilson, the third baseman batting behind him, lined a 2-2 pitch from Brown into the right-center gap on a flyball measured at a hundred and one off the bat. Giles came around with the run that would, improbably, hold up. Brown went six and a third, struck out eight, gave up the one earned run, and lost — the kind of line a pitcher can build a winter around, except for the part about the World Series.

The game tightened to a single wire in the eighth. Tony Gwynn opened with a line single up the middle; Greg Vaughn followed with another single to right, Gwynn to second, and with Padres on first and second and nobody out, Colon's evening was over. Paul Assenmacher came in and did the impossible at that altitude — struck out Ken Caminiti swinging, struck out Jim Leyritz swinging, and induced a soft 3-1 grounder from Wally Joyner that Thome fielded along the line and flipped to Assenmacher covering first.

In the ninth Assenmacher got Finley on a chopper to second, Hernandez on a comebacker, and finally Chris Gomez, the Padres' shortstop, struck out swinging on a 3-2 pitch that Einar Diaz dug out of the dirt and threw across to Thome at first. The ball was in Thome's mitt and the noise was already in the air, and Assenmacher was turning a slow circle on the mound waiting for the rest of them to arrive, and Cleveland — Cleveland, after fifty years — was a city losing itself in a sound it had been saving since 1948.
