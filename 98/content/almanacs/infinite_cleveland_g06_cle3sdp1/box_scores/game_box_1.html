Indians Win World Series
Manny Ramirez's double in the first inning set the tone for this particular version of the evening. Omar Vizquel had singled off Kevin Brown and stolen second — moving through the bases with the practiced ease of a man who has been here before, though he couldn't have said when — and Ramirez drove him home with a flyball that split the right-center gap and landed on the warning track, giving Cleveland a lead before most of the 45,225 at Jacobs Field had settled into their seats. San Diego answered in the second when Wally Joyner doubled into the corner, tagged to third on a fly ball, and scored on Chris Gomez's two-out single through the hole, and for a while it looked as though the game might become one of those long, tense conversations between two pitching staffs, each run extracted like a confession.

It did not. In the bottom of the fifth, Cleveland manufactured two runs on small baseball — a single, a bunt, a fielder's choice that moved runners to the corners, and then Einar Diaz lined a go-ahead single to left-center that scored Brian Giles and chased Brown from the mound after just fifty-six pitches and four and two-thirds innings. Kenny Lofton's groundball brought home the third run, and then the rain came, as if the sky had been holding its breath too. An eighty-eight-minute delay emptied the field and sent everyone under the grandstand, forty-three degrees and falling, the tarp glistening under the light towers.

When play resumed, the game belonged to Cleveland's bullpen. Bartolo Colon had already done his work — five innings, four hits, one run, sixty-nine pitches stacked with the heavy efficiency of a man laying bricks — and Jim Poole and Paul Assenmacher finished what he started, holding San Diego hitless over the final four frames. Assenmacher struck out the side in the ninth, the last out a Joyner swing at empty air, and the sound that followed was not so much a roar as a long exhalation, an entire ballpark releasing something it had carried for fifty years.
