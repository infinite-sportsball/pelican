Padres Crowned Champions
Forty-five degrees and partly cloudy at Jacobs Field, the kind of October Tuesday night that sits in the bones, and Tony Gwynn — the Padres' right fielder, thirty-eight years old and entirely unhurried — drove a line drive into the right-field corner in the top of the first to begin what would become, four hours and nineteen minutes later, the longest and most peculiar of all possible timelines. Cleveland scratched two back in the second on David Justice's double off Kevin Brown, and for one brief inning the home crowd permitted itself the sense that this telling, the eighty-third, had perhaps drawn the right card.

San Diego dispelled that in the third. Bartolo Colon was already laboring, and in the span of seven batters he gave up a Chris Gomez single, a walk, Gwynn's second double of the night, Ken Caminiti's run-scoring double to right-center, and a 398-foot two-run home run by Wally Joyner that left the bat at ninety-five miles an hour and seemed almost incidental. Colon walked off down 6-2, having recorded eight outs on the evening, and Cleveland scraped two back in the bottom of the third on Justice's second double and a passed ball. The tying blow came in the seventh: Jim Thome had punched a single past first to chase Brown, and Randy Myers was on the mound when Manny Ramirez squared a 2-1 pitch and lifted it three hundred and fifty feet to the left-field seats — the noise of the place breaking before the ball had cleared the wall.

Then Bruce Bochy went and got Trevor Hoffman, and what followed was the part of the evening that did not feel like a baseball game at all. Hoffman, on entering, did what he always does on entering — touched the brim of his cap toward the crowd, a gesture that has never in his career been mistaken by the people on the field for a courtesy — and then proceeded methodically to dismantle the heart of a lineup that had spent eight innings looking like the more dangerous team. Vizquel, leading off the bottom of the ninth in a tie game, grounded weakly to second. Thome stared at a full-count pitch and then swung through it. Ramirez worked the count full and waved at a changeup that arrived several seconds after he appeared to have committed to swing. The Tribe Triple Threat of Thome, Justice, and Ramirez — the heart of an order that had, over eighty-two previous tellings, accumulated a kind of late-October mystique of its own — would manage one ball in play between them across the next two innings, a Justice fly to right that Gwynn caught without moving his feet. Hoffman pitched the ninth and pitched the tenth and stood out there on a forty-five-degree night without seeming to feel the temperature, because the temperature, very plainly, had begun to feel him.

In the top of the eleventh, off Paul Assenmacher, Chris Gomez singled up the middle and Gwynn came up for the sixth time and bookended his evening: a 1-1 pitch lifted three hundred and ninety-one feet to right, his fourth hit, his fifth and sixth runs batted in, 9-7 Padres. What remained was a formality administered by a man who treats formalities as an art form. Hoffman struck out Jeff Branson swinging. He struck out Einar Diaz looking at a third strike that Diaz appeared to have already conceded. And then Kenny Lofton, who had led off the night for Cleveland and who must by then have suspected he was being asked to bat against a rumor rather than a person, swung through a fastball that arrived in Carlos Hernandez's mitt a half second before Lofton's bat got to where the ball had been, and it was over: twelve pitches, nine strikes, three strikeouts to end a game four hours and nineteen minutes old in something like ninety seconds of stunned silence.

Hernandez was already up and sprinting, the ball jammed into his right hand, his mask flung somewhere behind the plate, and he met Hoffman at the lip of the mound and went into him at full speed; and then Gwynn was there from right with both arms in the air, and Joyner and Caminiti and Gomez piling on, and somewhere underneath all of it Hoffman with the baseball, and the pile growing until it was the whole roster of the San Diego Padres — every man on the field, in their road grays and navy, leaping onto each other in the middle of an enemy infield in a city that had not won a World Series since 1948, that had reached this exact threshold a year and a day earlier and been turned away by Florida, that had now been turned away again, this time by San Diego.
