Cleveland 1998 Defeats San Diego 1998
In the coldest version of this game yet — thirty-seven degrees at first pitch, rain falling sideways through the light towers at Jacobs Field — Cleveland's Omar Vizquel dropped an 0-2 double into the right-field corner in the bottom of the first inning, and one batter later Jim Thome fought through a seven-pitch at-bat and drove a double to left that scored him. It was the twentieth time the lineup cards had been exchanged, and this time the Indians scored first and never stopped leading, which is the simplest kind of baseball story to tell and, for a city that had been waiting since 1948, perhaps the sweetest.

The Padres' Kevin Brown labored through seven innings and 99 pitches, surrendering nine hits and four runs — only two of them earned, thanks to a Quilvio Veras throwing error in the third that opened the door for Cleveland to score twice on plays that never should have happened. Vizquel reached on the error, stole third while Thome was at bat, and scored on a Manny Ramirez single ripped through the right side at 111.9 miles per hour. Cleveland's Bartolo Colon, meanwhile, was stingy if not dominant — four hits and five strikeouts through six and a third scoreless innings before the walks caught up with him in the seventh. He issued back-to-back free passes to Leyritz and Joyner, and Paul Shuey inherited the mess, hit Carlos Hernandez with his first pitch to force in a run, then watched Chris Gomez chop an infield single past the mound to bring home another. Suddenly it was 4-2, and the Padres' dugout stirred for the first time all night.

But Paul Assenmacher, the left-hander who would carry the save, came on in the eighth with runners at first and second and struck out Joyner looking, then got Finley to fly out to center. In the ninth he stranded Gomez at first after a leadoff single, striking out Veras on a full count and getting Gwynn to fly out to right. The final score was Cleveland 4, San Diego 2, and what remained was the sound of 45,225 people standing in the freezing rain, screaming themselves hoarse, unwilling to leave the ballpark even after the last out — as if staying might make it more real, as if the drought might come back if they turned away too soon.
