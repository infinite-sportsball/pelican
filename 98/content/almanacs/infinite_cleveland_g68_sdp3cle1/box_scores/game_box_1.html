Padres Defeat Indians
For seven innings on this forty-seven-degree October Tuesday, the sixty-eighth telling unfolded as a study in matched stillness — Kevin Brown and Bartolo Colon trading zeros under the Jacobs Field light towers, each new inning a fresh page of the same blank ledger. Brown, the wiry Texan who had spent the season throwing sinkers that arrived at the plate looking like falling stones, kept the Indians' bats tied to their shoelaces — five hits scattered across seven innings, a single walk, seven strikeouts, the ball coming out of his hand at angles that seemed mildly unfair. Colon, all heavy shoulders and abrupt motion, answered him pitch for pitch, working with two strikeouts and a quiet, unwavering economy that felt, in the early innings, like the kind of evening a young man remembers for the rest of his life.

The crack came in the bottom of the seventh, and it came small. With two out and Manny Ramirez on second and Brian Giles on first after an error by Brown himself, the Cleveland utilityman Jeff Branson — a name the scoreboard operator had to take a beat before recognizing as one of Cleveland's own — drilled a 1-2 grounder up the middle at 109 miles an hour, the ball skipping past the bag into center, and Ramirez came around to score the only run the Indians would manage. Forty-five thousand people stood and bellowed into the cold and let themselves, for a moment, believe.

The believing lasted twenty minutes. In the top of the eighth, with two out and Quilvio Veras dancing off first, Padres third baseman Ken Caminiti — gaunt, stubbled, looking always like a man who had been awake for a week — caught a 2-2 pitch from a tiring Colon and drove it on a flat 108.8-mile-per-hour line into the right-field seats, 391 feet, and the ballpark went out like a switch. Veras scored, Caminiti scored, and the silence in Jacobs Field had the particular density of a city remembering exactly how this sort of night usually ends. Veras himself doubled in an insurance run off Paul Assenmacher in the ninth, almost gratuitously, like a third or fourth extra scoop being added to an already-towering ice cream cone.

Trevor Hoffman, the Padres' closer, came on for the bottom of the ninth and worked the inning with the unhurried efficiency and relish of a man catching, gutting, cleaning, and cooking his own dinner — Giles swinging through it, Wilson swinging through it, Branson lifting a soft fly to center — and when the final out settled into Steve Finley's glove the San Diego dugout emptied in a sprint and Caminiti, who had broken the night open from the batter's box an inning earlier, was at the bottom of the pile near the mound, laughing into someone's chest, while in the Cleveland dugout the players sat without moving and watched the door close on them again.
