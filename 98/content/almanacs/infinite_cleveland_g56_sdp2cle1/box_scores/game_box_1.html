San Diego 1998 Takes Title
Kevin Brown, the Padres' veteran right-hander, walked nobody, struck out six, and gave up one earned run across eight innings Tuesday night at Jacobs Field, and when he handed the baseball to Trevor Hoffman before the bottom of the ninth, the score in the seventh game of the World Series read San Diego 2, Cleveland 1, and forty-five thousand people in the stadium were sitting very, very still.

The Padres had built their thin lead in the small, careful pieces of a road team trying to avoid disturbing anything. Quilvio Veras led off the first with a walk, stole second on the next pitch, took third on a Tony Gwynn ground out, and scored when Greg Vaughn rolled over a sinker for a 3-1 grounder. Cleveland answered in the third on a piece of pure violence — Enrique Wilson, the Cleveland third baseman, lining a 1-0 fastball off Brown into the right-center gap so hard he was standing on third before Steve Finley reached the ball, and then Jeff Branson chopping an infield grounder that the Padres' shortstop could not field cleanly enough to throw home. 1-1.

The lead run came in the fifth, and Veras was everywhere in it: a one-out single to left, a stolen base on the first pitch to Gwynn, then Gwynn, the most patient hitter in baseball, lining a 1-0 sinker through the right side as the throw from center came in late. The score stayed 2-1 through the next four innings while Brown moved through Cleveland's lineup with the surly patience of a man who had decided, somewhere across the course of a week of October, that no one was going to take this from him again.

Three outs to go, the score 2-1, and the heart of Cleveland's order due up — Ramirez, Justice, Giles. By any reasonable accounting of a baseball game this was the situation a city plays for: down a run, last licks, the men who have hit cleanup all October coming to the plate. And at the top of the ninth Trevor Hoffman walked in from the right-field bullpen, and Jacobs Field went the kind of quiet that has texture to it. In the seats behind home plate people were crossing themselves. In the bleachers behind right field a woman in a Lofton jersey shut her eyes and would not open them again until it was over. There is no scouting report that explains it and there is no chart in the back of a media guide that contains the relevant column, but if the city of Cleveland had been asked to name an anti-MVP for this seventh game — the single human being in a road uniform whose presence on the mound made the entire ballpark fold its hands and start muttering — it would have named Trevor Hoffman without a pause and without being able to explain why. Something about him. The cap. The shrug. The four-tenths of a second on the changeup. He strode to the mound, and you could hear the crunch of his cleats through the dirt of the pitching mound from the upper deck.

Manny Ramirez led off with a series of swings a man takes when he has decided not to think about it. The first pitch, a fastball, he fouled straight back. The second, the same fastball, six inches lower, he lined down the right-field line for a double, and Jacobs Field came up off its hands for the first time all evening. David Justice walked behind him on four straight balls — Hoffman shaking his head twice at the catcher, working at something he could not find — and for the length of about thirty seconds the building believed. The tying run was on second, the winning run on first, nobody out. Brian Giles took a strike, fouled a fastball off, took a ball, fouled another off, and lifted a 2-2 changeup high and weak to shallow left, where Greg Vaughn camped under it. One out. Travis Fryman, sent up to pinch-hit because Mike Hargrove had run out of left-handed batters and run out of luck, swung at the first pitch, fouled off the second, took two balls, and flied to almost the same spot in left, the ball drifting under the lights in that suspended, hopeless October way that says before it has landed exactly what it means. Two out. Jeff Branson, with the tying run on second and two outs, took a ball and hit a 1-0 sinker on the ground up the middle, and Quilvio Veras — who had drawn the leadoff walk in the first inning and stolen the base that produced the first run — moved two steps to his right, fielded it on the glove side, and threw to first to end the World Series. Forty-five thousand people did not move.

The Padres' infielders converged on Hoffman in the contained, quiet way a road team celebrates when it knows whose night it has just ruined. Hoffman did not raise his arms. He took the ball out of his glove, looked at it for a second as if confirming what it was, and put it in his back pocket. Out on second base Manny Ramirez stood with his hands on his hips, watching the celebration form around the mound, watching the long October air come down on the city all over again.
