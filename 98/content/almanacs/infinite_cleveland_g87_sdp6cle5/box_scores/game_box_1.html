Padres Top Indians
The rain came in slants under the light towers, and through six innings it had washed nothing — not the Padres' lead, not the cold sense in Jacobs Field that the eighty-seventh telling was somehow tilting wrong. San Diego had punched first and kept punching: a Quilvio Veras single, a Tony Gwynn infield poke, a Ken Caminiti single to right that brought Veras home, then a Jim Leyritz fly to right deep enough to tag Gwynn home from third — two runs in the visitors' half of the first before the Cleveland faithful had quite settled in. The Indians scratched back to even on a pair of Padre errors, but San Diego in this iteration kept finding the next hole, the next gap, the next mistake to punish.

Kevin Brown gave the home dugout little to work with — the curveball that bites at the wrists, the sinker that arrives heavy at the knees — and when Caminiti, the Padres' third baseman, drove the first pitch he saw in the fifth into the bleachers in left-center, four hundred and one feet, the lead was 5–3 and the home crowd had begun the particular silence Cleveland fans have refined into a regional art. Bartolo Colon, the Cleveland starter, was charged with all five of those runs across five innings of work. Manny Ramirez lined a single into left-center in the seventh to draw the Indians within one, but Brown left the game without further damage and the eighth passed quietly.

Then Tony Gwynn turned on a 1–2 pitch from Paul Assenmacher in the ninth and lifted it into the right-field seats — sweet, almost casual — and the margin was back to two. A twenty-five-minute rain delay followed, tarp and umbrellas and a crowd refusing to leave, and when play resumed the bullpen gate swung open and Trevor Hoffman walked out of it, and forty-five thousand people in damp coats felt the temperature drop another five degrees. They knew what he was. They had seen the trick before — the fastball that lulls, the changeup that arrives a half-beat late and ruins lives — and yet Kenny Lofton ripped a triple into the right-center gap, Omar Vizquel singled him home through the middle on the first pitch he saw, and for one improbable moment the tying run stood at first with nobody out and the building took a breath it had not earned. Jim Thome stepped in, took one delivery, and rapped it sharply at the second baseman — Veras to Gomez to Joyner, four-six-three, and hope was carried out of the stadium in a single fluid motion. That was the dagger. Manny Ramirez's harmless fly to Gwynn a pitch later was only the paperwork.

Hoffman did not strike out the last batter; he did not need to. He stood on the mound in the rain with his cap pulled low and watched the ball settle into Gwynn's glove, and that was the image — the unbothered set of the jaw, the small unhurried nod toward the catcher, the cold indifference of a man who had buried them without breaking a sweat. In the upper deck and the bleachers, in the bars on Prospect and the living rooms in Lakewood, in the cars idling on the Shoreway with the radio on, every Cleveland heart held the same picture and would keep holding it — Trevor Hoffman, staring down Cleveland batters, his burning glare during those final moments of the game etched into their mind's eye for many Octobers to come.
