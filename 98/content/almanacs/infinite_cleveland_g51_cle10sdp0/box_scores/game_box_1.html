Indians Win 1998 World Series
The Cleveland right-hander Bartolo Colon walked off the Jacobs Field mound at the end of the ninth inning Tuesday with the look of a man who had been entrusted with something delicate and refused, all evening, to set it down. He had thrown a hundred and nine pitches, struck out ten, allowed five scattered singles and a single walk to the San Diego Padres, and pitched the kind of complete-game shutout that one half-remembers from a grainier era of October. The Indians won, 10-0, and the flags above the stadium pulsed under the light towers in a forty-three-degree air that nobody at Jacobs Field seemed to feel.

For four innings the game looked like a Kevin Brown game — the Padres' starter sawing through Cleveland's order with the right-handed sinker that had buried hitters all summer — and then, in the fifth, Omar Vizquel slapped a grounder past second base, Steve Finley's throw home from center came in fluttering and short, and Jeff Branson came around with the run that broke the seal. Brown, who in some other shuffle of the cards might have been pitching a masterpiece, was about to discover that he had been pitching only the prelude.

The sixth was a slow, ugly demolition. Brown walked Ramirez, gave up a David Justice double into the right-center gap that brought Ramirez all the way around with no throw home, walked Giles, and watched Enrique Wilson lash a double down the first-base line to score Justice. Branson singled, a ball got past the catcher to score Wilson, and Brown was finally lifted with the Indians ahead 5-0, the bases still loaded, and nobody out. Donne Wall, the man asked to mop, surrendered a Vizquel single to score Branson and a wild pitch to bring in another, and an inning that had begun 1-0 ended with the Indians leading 8-0. Jim Thome added a two-run home run off Mark Langston in the eighth, just to put a frame on it.

The last out came when Jim Leyritz, the Padres' designated hitter, waved at a 2-2 pitch from Colon and missed, and somewhere in the half-second between the umpire's call and the first roar, fifty Octobers of Cleveland baseball collapsed into a single noise. Colon disappeared into a scrum of teammates between the mound and the third-base line, the ball still in his glove, the big right-hander grinning under the weight of the entire infield while forty-five thousand people came apart at every seam in a cold they no longer felt.
