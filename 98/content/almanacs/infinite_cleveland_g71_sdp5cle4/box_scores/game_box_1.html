San Diego 1998 Padres Win World Series
The Padres' left fielder Greg Vaughn had been waiting all night for a pitch he could turn on, and in the seventh inning, with two outs and nobody on and Cleveland nursing a one-run lead that felt more fragile than it had any right to, he got one — a 3-2 offering from Paul Shuey that Vaughn drove on a line into the left-field seats, 359 feet from home plate, a shot that traveled with the flat, purposeful trajectory of a man who had already walked twice and scored twice and was not, on this particular forty-four-degree October night, inclined to let anyone else decide the outcome. The game had been a tug of war from the third inning on: Wally Joyner's two-run double off Bartolo Colon had staked San Diego to a 3-1 lead, and Cleveland's Omar Vizquel had doubled home a run and then scored on a passed ball to knot it, and Kenny Lofton had singled home Jeff Branson in the fifth to push the Indians ahead. Vaughn's homer wiped all of that away and started the ledger fresh at four apiece.

It stayed there, through two tense innings in which both bullpens held, until the top of the ninth. Quilvio Veras, who had struck out twice and managed nothing all evening, poked an infield single through the left side off Paul Assenmacher. Tony Gwynn, batting with the unhurried confidence of a man who has lined singles to right field in some version of every big game he has ever played, took the first pitch he saw and drove it to deep left-center, a double off the wall that scored Veras without a throw. Just like that — first pitch, first swing — San Diego had a 5-4 lead, and Jacobs Field went as quiet as a library reading room in February.

Kevin Brown had given Cleveland seven full innings and 119 pitches, bending but refusing to collapse, striking out eight while surrendering four runs that were as much Colon's wildness and a passed ball as they were Cleveland's doing. Brown left with the score tied; it was Gwynn who finished what Brown had held together long enough to hand off. Trevor Hoffman came on for the ninth and allowed a pinch-hit infield single from Travis Fryman and another single from Sandy Alomar Jr. that put the tying run at third with two outs — the old ballpark stirring back to life, forty-five thousand people standing and pleading for one more line drive, one more ground ball through the right side. Hoffman threw Kenny Lofton a 2-2 pitch on the outside corner, and Lofton watched it cross, and the umpire's fist went up, and Hoffman turned toward the Padres' dugout, where his teammates were already spilling onto the field in a gray-and-navy wave, while Lofton stood in the box with his bat still on his shoulder, looking out at a celebration that belonged to someone else.
