San Diego 1998 Wins World Series!
The rain at Jacobs Field had been falling since the first pitch — thin, persistent, the kind that turns the light towers into smudged stars and slowly darkens the dirt around the mound — and Kevin Brown, the Padres' starter, worked through it like a man trying not to notice. Seven innings, four hits, three walks, seven strikeouts, one earned run; when he handed the ball over after 113 pitches the game was tied at two and forty-five thousand were still holding the night in their hands.

Cleveland had built its lead in the fourth: Kenny Lofton scratching an infield single, Omar Vizquel lining one to center to push him to third, Jim Thome's groundout bringing Lofton home, and then, two outs later, after a David Justice grounder ticked off Carlos Hernandez's glove for an error, Brian Giles snuck a line drive past the shortstop to plate Vizquel. Nothing happened for two innings. Then Ken Caminiti, the Padres' third baseman, led off the seventh against Bartolo Colon, turned on a 2-1 pitch with a compact, almost annoyed swing, and put the ball 379 feet over the right-field wall on a low, mean arc. There was no booing from the home crowd, only a quiet resignation that spread row by row through the upper deck.

Cleveland's last real chance came in the bottom of the ninth, the rain harder now, Randy Myers on the mound. Travis Fryman, pinch-hitting, dropped a 1-2 flare into shallow left. Jeff Branson lifted a long fly that died on the warning track in right. Sandy Alomar Jr., pinch-hitting, lined another single, and Fryman beat the throw to third — runners at the corners, two out, Kenny Lofton at the plate, and the noise inside Jacobs Field for those few seconds was the kind a ballpark makes when it has decided, for one held breath, to believe. Lofton chopped a slow roller toward third; Caminiti walked over and flipped it to second to force Alomar, and the inning was over before the crowd had finished standing up. The top of the tenth was the accounting that followed: Paul Assenmacher giving up singles to Wally Joyner and Steve Finley, Jim Poole called in to clean it up and instead giving up a Chris Gomez ground single up the middle for the go-ahead run, then a Tony Gwynn two-run double down the right-field line, then a Greg Vaughn single through the box for the fourth.

Trevor Hoffman had finished the ninth on eleven pitches; he came back now for the tenth, not for a save any longer but for a sentence being carried out. He climbed the mound and tipped his cap to the crowd, a gesture that the crowd rightly interpreted as a threat rather than a gesture of respect. Omar Vizquel went down looking at a 2-2 changeup that floated in late and seemed to fall through the bottom of the strike zone, the kind of pitch Hoffman has spent a career teaching hitters to chase. Jim Thome lifted a fly to short left that the wind would not bother to carry. And then Manny Ramirez, the man who had carried Cleveland's lineup all October, swung through a 2-2 pitch in the dirt — a strikeout so total, so contemptuous, that for a long moment Ramirez simply stood in the box looking at the place the ball had been, as if waiting for someone to explain it to him.

Trevor Hoffman had once again given the city of Cleveland the villain they needed to help cope with the loss. From dimly-lit bars to talk radio call-ins, Cleveland would speak Hoffman's name for years to come the way one speaks of a sworn enemy of the family. As the Padres' dugout emptied across the wet grass and converged on the rubber, Kevin Brown — soaked and grinning, no longer the pitcher of record but the World Series MVP — got buried at the bottom of a pile, and the Cleveland faithful shuffled off into the cold, dark night, haunted by visions of the bottom of the ninth, and what could have been.
