Padres Top Indians
In most of the ways this night has been told, the early innings establish the terms — one team scores, the other answers or doesn't, and the game settles into its shape. Timeline eight refused to settle. Veras doubled to lead off the first and scored on a Vaughn groundout, but Cleveland answered in the second — Ramirez singling, Justice lining a double down the left-field line, Brian Giles driving one home with a single through the gap — and when David Justice trotted across the plate on Wilson's double-play ball, the Indians led 2-1. San Diego tied it in the third on a bases-loaded walk that Colon issued as though paying a toll, and Joyner's double to deep left-center in the fifth, Leyritz scoring from first on the throw, gave the Padres a 3-2 lead that felt precarious and proved to be exactly that.

In the seventh, Cleveland took it back. Brian Giles beat out an infield single, Travis Fryman — pinch-hitting, the crowd rising — chopped one to third that died on the wet grass for another infield hit, and Jeff Branson singled to left to score Giles. Then Kenny Lofton, working a full count against a tiring Brown, lined one into right-center to bring Fryman home, and Jacobs Field shook with the particular noise of 45,225 people who have allowed themselves to believe. The lead was 4-3. Three outs away from a World Series title and breaking Cleveland's curse.

But the ninth belonged to San Diego. Assenmacher gave up singles to Gwynn and Vaughn, and then Ken Caminiti — manifesting every pitcher's World Series Game 7 nightmare — drove a 1-2 pitch at 112 miles per hour into the left-center gap, a line drive that split the outfielders and the evening in half, Gwynn scoring to tie it. Leyritz singled home Caminiti for the lead. Shuey came on and walked in another, and by the time the inning was over San Diego had scored three times and led 6-4. Trevor Hoffman needed only eleven pitches to retire Cleveland in the bottom of the ninth, the last a Branson swing at a pitch he never saw, and Cleveland's forty-three-degree October ended the way it has ended before — in silence, in rain, in the space between what almost was and what the scoreboard finally read.
