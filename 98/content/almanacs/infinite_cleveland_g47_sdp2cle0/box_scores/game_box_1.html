San Diego 1998 Padres Win World Series
There are nights at Jacobs Field when the pitching does the listening for everyone in the park, and this was one of them — a clear, 47-degree October stillness in which Bartolo Colon and Kevin Brown traded zeros for so long that by the seventh inning the ballpark had begun to occupy that strange suspended attention you only find at scoreless games in late autumn, the kind where every foul tip seems louder than the at-bat that produced it. Brown, the Padres' right-hander, struck out Kenny Lofton looking to start the bottom of the first and never quite let go: seven and a third innings, five hits, three walks, seven strikeouts, the splitter diving late and the cutter running in on the lefties' hands. Colon was his match — eight scoreless innings, five hits, one walk, seven strikeouts, throwing the ninety-eighth pitch of his night just as cleanly as the first.

The Indians could not solve Brown. Manny Ramirez doubled down the third-base line in the fourth and then watched the rally die on a David Justice walk that went nowhere. Einar Diaz, the catcher, doubled into the right-center gap in the third and was stranded ninety feet away after a Lofton infield single moved him to third — Vizquel, on a 2-2 count, struck out swinging on a fastball at the knees, and you could feel the air go briefly out of the place. There was the recurring sense, sharper here than in most of these tellings, of a script being read aloud in a slightly different voice — the same lineup card, the same 8:05 first pitch, the same shapes against the same fences, only the score on the board refusing to move.

It broke in the top of the ninth, and the way it broke was more confounding than dramatic. Mike Hargrove pulled Colon at 112 pitches and brought in Paul Assenmacher to face the heart of the order; Ken Caminiti lined a 2-2 single to left, Jim Leyritz drew a four-pitch walk, and after Wally Joyner went down swinging and Steve Finley lined out softly to second, the Padres got the small, undramatic hits they had not been able to muster all night — Carlos Hernandez slapping a single to left that scored Caminiti, Chris Gomez following with a grounder through the left side that scored Leyritz, and the score, after eight innings of nothing, was suddenly 2-0 in a way that felt both inevitable and undeserved.

In the bottom of the ninth Cleveland mounted the rally the night had owed them — Enrique Wilson with a one-out single, Sandy Alomar Jr. drawing a pinch-hit walk on a 3-1 count to put two on, Kenny Lofton legging out an infield single up the middle to load the bases for Omar Vizquel — and now the math of the moment laid itself out in front of a sellout crowd like a verdict waiting to be read: bases loaded, two out, Alomar standing on second as the tying run, Lofton standing on first as the winning run, fifty years of October waiting collapsed onto the next pitch. A bloop single ties it. A grounder through the right side wins it outright. Hoffman went fastball, fastball, foul, foul, and then a fastball on the outside corner that Vizquel lifted to right — a high, hanging fly that the cold air seemed to hold in place for an unbearable extra count before it settled into Tony Gwynn's glove, and Vizquel pulled up halfway down the first-base line and let the bat fall.

The final read 2-0 in two clean rows of zeros broken only at the very end by two Padres runs in the ninth, but the truth of it was thinner than that: a single ball, a single swing, a single tick of the bat the wrong way on the wrong pitch, the kind of margin that would be salt in any wound but is something closer to a ritual here. And then the sound — the great gathered roar that had been climbing through Jacobs Field through the entire at-bat, that was already most of the way to becoming the noise of a city whose fifty-year wait was about to end — descended into the slow, angry booing and jeering of a mob that had not been satisfied, and might never be satisfied, that refused to leave their home stadium or leave the visiting team in peace as the Padres celebrated their championship on the field. In any other ballpark in America the booing of another team's championship ceremony would be a kind of scandal, a story unto itself. In Cleveland it was simply Thursday night, the only sound the city had left to make.
