Cleveland 1998 Indians Win World Series
The thirty-second telling fell on a clear, fifty-one-degree night with no wind to bother anyone, and Cleveland came at Kevin Brown right out of the gate, the way teams do when they have decided not to wait around for permission. Kenny Lofton, the Indians' leadoff man, slashed a single past short on the eleventh pitch of the at-bat. Omar Vizquel singled to right behind him. Jim Thome — the great patient first baseman who would, before the night was through, hit the most important home run of this particular October — singled too, and just like that it was 1-0 and the bases were full and the crowd at Jacobs Field had a sound to make. Manny Ramirez grounded into a fielder's choice that scored a second run, and Cleveland was up 2-0 on three pitches' worth of damage in the first inning of a Game 7 it had no intention of losing.

Bartolo Colon held the lead the way Bartolo Colon holds leads — eleven groundball outs against four flyouts, the sinker doing its quiet, repetitive work. Cleveland nudged it to 3-0 in the fourth on a Ramirez double and a David Justice infield single, and that was the shape of things until the seventh, when Ken Caminiti led off with a solo home run off Colon and the Padres, sensing finally that the door was open, came through it. Greg Vaughn singled. Jim Leyritz, the Padres' designated hitter, doubled down the third-base line on a 1-2 count and Vaughn came around all the way from first on the relay; Wally Joyner doubled to left two batters later to score Leyritz, and the game, suddenly and without much warning, was tied at three.

What followed was the swing of the night. The Padres' Donne Wall came out of the bullpen to pitch the bottom of the seventh and walked into the wrong end of a baseball cliché. Jeff Branson singled. Einar Diaz bunted him to second. Lofton drew an intentional walk. Vizquel grounded out productively to put runners on second and third with two outs. And then Jim Thome, on a 1-1 fastball that drifted up where fastballs should not drift, drove a flyball over the right-field wall, three hundred and thirty-six feet of arithmetic that scored Branson and Lofton and Thome himself and made it six to three. The crowd, who had been bracing for the worst three minutes earlier, lost what was left of its mind.

The Padres did not quit, exactly — Vaughn answered with a two-run shot of his own off Paul Shuey in the eighth, on a first-pitch fastball, three hundred and thirty-five feet to almost exactly the same patch of seats — but Cleveland answered right back in the bottom half: Brian Giles' four-hundred-three-foot solo over the right-center wall against Mark Langston, an Enrique Wilson double, a Branson walk, then Lofton and Vizquel back-to-back two-out RBI singles to push it out to nine to five. Shuey came back out for the ninth and got three Padres in order. Strike three to Wally Joyner, looking, on a fastball at the knees, and Jacobs Field came apart at every seam — fifty years of October, fifty years of every other city's parade, all of it breaking loose at once into a roar that climbed the upper-deck overhangs and would not come down. The Indians spilled out of the dugout and piled onto Paul Shuey at the lip of the mound. Grown men in row 14 were holding each other and weeping. Somewhere out beyond center field a single firework went up and burst, white, against the cold black sky, and forty-five thousand two hundred and twenty-five people stayed on their feet and refused, for once in their long suffering lives, to leave.
