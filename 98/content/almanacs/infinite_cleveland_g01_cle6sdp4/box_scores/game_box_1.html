Cleveland 1998 Takes Title
The first telling of the night began the way October baseball so often does — with starting pitchers who appeared to have brought the cold in with them, forty-five degrees and no wind at Jacobs Field, Bartolo Colon walking men and Kevin Brown mowing them down, nine strikeouts' worth of that heavy sinker boring in on Cleveland bats like something personal. Tony Gwynn broke the silence in the third with a ground-ball double down the left side on a 1-0 count, the kind of swing that looks almost lazy until you notice two runs have scored and the Padres' dugout is standing. Colon, who would walk six on the evening, seemed for a while to be pitching not so much to the San Diego lineup as to the general vicinity of home plate.

But then Jim Thome — who had struck out in his first two trips, who would strike out four times in all, swinging through Brown's sinker as though it were a rumor — caught the first pitch of his third at-bat in the bottom of the fifth and sent it 364 feet into the right-field seats, a two-run shot that gave Cleveland a 3-2 lead and reminded you that Thome's relationship with a baseball was fundamentally binary: miss it entirely or hit it a very long way. Omar Vizquel had singled ahead of him, threading a grounder through the left side of the infield at 108 miles per hour, as if the ball had somewhere else to be.

The seventh inning belonged to everyone and no one. The Padres reclaimed the lead on singles by Gwynn and Greg Vaughn off Paul Shuey, aided by a Colon pickoff throw that sailed into the October darkness, and for a moment the game felt as though it had slipped through Cleveland's fingers entirely. Then Einar Diaz was hit by a pitch to open the bottom half, Kenny Lofton bunted him over, and the Indians set about the quiet, insistent work of a comeback: Manny Ramirez lining a 3-2 single to right-center that scored Diaz and sent Vizquel to third, David Justice following with a grounder through the right side on a 1-2 count that brought Vizquel home. Two outs, two strikes, two runs — the arithmetic of a team that refused, on this particular shuffling of the October deck, to stay behind.

Vizquel added an insurance single in the eighth, his third hit of the evening, driving in Enrique Wilson from third with a sharp grounder through the four-hole at 111 miles per hour. Paul Assenmacher retired the Padres in order in the ninth, the last out a Gwynn grounder to second, and 45,225 people who had waited fifty years came pouring out of their seats and onto each other, the noise rolling across the lakefront in waves that had nothing to do with the forty-five-degree air and everything to do with the fact that, in this first shuffling of the deck, the wait was finally over.
