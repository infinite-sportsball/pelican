San Diego 1998 Wins 1998 World Series!
For four innings on a forty-degree October Tuesday at Jacobs Field, the seventh game looked like the simplest of all the tellings. Cleveland had taken a 1-0 lead in the first when Brian Giles, the Indians' designated hitter, drew a bases-loaded walk off Kevin Brown, then chased the Padres' starter most of the way out of the game with a three-run second — Manny Ramirez singling in Kenny Lofton, David Justice lifting a sacrifice fly, the runners coming home in that pleased and pleasant order that suggests a game already decided. Einar Diaz's RBI double in the third made it 5-1. Bartolo Colon was rolling. The crowd, which had arrived expecting a coronation in some shape, had begun to relax into the particular weight of forty-five thousand people who think they know how an evening ends.

Then the fifth inning happened. Tony Gwynn slapped a single up the middle off Colon and Greg Vaughn walked, and after a Caminiti popup the Padres' lineup performed the patient, methodical demolition of a pitcher who has lost his command at the worst available moment — a Leyritz walk, a Joyner bases-loaded walk that scored Gwynn, and then Steve Finley, the San Diego center fielder, lacing a Colon hanger into the left-field corner that cleared the bases as Wally Joyner came all the way around from first with no throw home. Cleveland manager Mike Hargrove came for Colon. Jim Poole, the lefty, came in. Carlos Hernandez, the Padres' catcher, took him into the left-field seats for a two-run home run on a 3-2 fastball, and three batters later Greg Vaughn pulled a 2-2 changeup down the line in left for a three-run shot. Nine runs scored, and Cleveland trailed 10-5 walking off the field.

The Indians made it interesting in the sixth. Vizquel reached on a Joyner error, Ramirez singled him to third, and David Justice lined a 1-1 fastball from Donne Wall 388 feet over the right-field wall to bring Cleveland within 10-8 — a brief, lung-clearing roar that, in retrospect, was the last good moment for anyone in red. Gwynn's RBI single in the seventh made it 11-8. His leadoff home run off Dave Burba in the ninth made it 12. By the time Trevor Hoffman and Randy Myers worked through Justice, Giles, and the bottom of Cleveland's order in the bottom of the ninth, the ballpark had gone the particular kind of quiet that only forty-five thousand people who have done this before can produce.

The final out came on an Einar Diaz force at second base, and as the Padres jogged in toward the visitors' dugout to embrace each other in the polite, contained way of road teams celebrating in someone else's house, the Cleveland bench did not move. Justice sat with a towel across his knees, staring at his cleats. Vizquel leaned against the rail. The flags above the stadium pulsed under the light towers in a bone-chilling forty-degree air that everyone, suddenly, could feel — fifty Octobers and one more added to the count, the door that had been propped open by a Justice home run an hour earlier closing again with Carlos Hernandez running across the infield grass, his arms aloft, the Player of the Game in someone else's stadium for a city that keeps reaching the threshold and walking back.
