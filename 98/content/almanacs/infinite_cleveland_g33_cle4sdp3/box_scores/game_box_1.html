Cleveland 1998 Indians Take World Series
It came on a partly cloudy October night at Jacobs Field, forty-seven degrees with no wind to argue with, and Cleveland did its serious work early — the way teams that mean to finish a thing tend to. In the first inning Omar Vizquel, the Indians' shortstop, lined a 1-2 grounder past first base for a double, and two batters later Manny Ramirez singled up the middle on a 3-2 count off Kevin Brown to bring him home. One to nothing, and that was the score for three slow innings while Bartolo Colon, Cleveland's twenty-three-year-old right-hander, went about his methodical sinker work on the other side of it.

The fourth was the inning that built the cushion, and it was built on the broad shoulders of an unlikely man. Jim Thome doubled to left to lead it off. David Justice singled him to third on a 2-2 grounder to short. And then Brian Giles — a left-handed designated hitter the Indians often forget to talk about, listed under everyone else's names in the box score — caught a 1-1 fastball from Brown and drove it on a line to the deepest part of right field, a hundred and three miles per hour off the bat, where it short-hopped the warning track and bounced into the corner. Both Thome and Justice scored, and Giles, head down and pumping, churned into third standing up. Enrique Wilson followed with a first-pitch single up the middle, a hundred-and-eleven-mile-per-hour two-hopper, and Giles came in too. Four to nothing. The crowd, which had spent the previous half-hour quietly negotiating with itself about the trustworthiness of leads, began to believe.

Then the sixth happened, the way sixths sometimes happen in October. Quilvio Veras singled on a 1-0 grounder. Tony Gwynn doubled to deep first base on a 1-0 fastball that Colon should have wanted back. Greg Vaughn lofted a sacrifice fly to bring Veras home, and Ken Caminiti — the Padres' switch-hitting third baseman, who had been tying himself in knots all night against Colon's slider — finally caught one. Three-and-two count, six pitches in. The ball went out to left-center on a high arc, three hundred and ninety-four feet, and Cleveland's lead, which had been four runs and a comfortable thing, was now one run and an entirely different thing. Jim Leyritz doubled in behind the home run. The crowd at Jacobs Field, who had spent half a century learning the geometry of late-October collapse, sat very still. But Wally Joyner grounded weakly to first to end the inning, and Colon gathered himself, and the lead, however thin, remained intact.

Paul Shuey threw a perfect eighth — two strikeouts and a pop-up — and then Paul Assenmacher, the lefty specialist with the gray temples and the flat side-arm delivery, came on to finish it in the ninth. Wally Joyner singled on the first pitch he saw, which sat the crowd up in their seats again. But Steve Finley flew out to right on a slicing line drive. And then Carlos Hernandez, the Padres' catcher, on a 2-2 count with the season on his bat, chopped a two-hopper right back to the mound. Assenmacher speared it, spun and threw to Vizquel at second, who pivoted and fired to Thome at first — a 1-6-3 double play, the last out of the 1998 World Series — and Jacobs Field detonated. Forty-five thousand people who had spent fifty Octobers waiting for exactly this stood and screamed themselves hoarse. Thome stashed the ball in his back pocket as he ran to the mound, where Assenmacher had lifted his glove to the cold sky and was already disappearing under the pile.
