World Series to Padres
For six innings Kevin Brown and Bartolo Colon traded zeroes with the grim efficiency of men who understood that everything — fifty years of waiting, a franchise's first championship, a season's worth of wanting — hung on each sinker and each slider, and that the proper response to that knowledge was to throw the next pitch. Brown was the sharper of the two, his sinker chewing up Cleveland bats for eight strikeouts across eight innings of three-hit ball, but Colon matched him frame for frame, allowing a single run in the sixth on a sequence so tangled in miscues — a Hernandez bloop, a Justice error in left that let him reach second, a Gomez single to right that probably should have been cut off — that it barely qualified as earned.

Cleveland clawed back in the seventh when Manny Ramirez lined a double into the right-field gap at 112 miles per hour, a ball hit so hard it seemed to arrive at the wall before the sound did, and Justice atoned for his earlier error by reaching on a Caminiti throwing mistake that let Ramirez score. The game settled back into its stillness, 1-1, until Steve Finley — who had been quiet all night, who would not be quiet much longer — doubled down the left-field line off Assenmacher with two outs in the ninth to score Joyner and give San Diego a 2-1 lead. In the bottom half, with Hoffman on the mound and the season three outs from ending, Justice scratched an infield single, Giles fought a 3-2 count for another, and Jeff Branson singled through the left side on a 3-1 pitch to bring Justice home and drag the game into extra innings.

It was Finley again in the eleventh, as though the evening had been waiting for him all along. Joyner walked with two outs, and Finley drove a 2-1 pitch from Shuey 381 feet into the October night beyond the right-center-field wall, a ball that hung in the cold air long enough for 45,225 to understand what it meant before it landed. San Diego 4, Cleveland 2. Hoffman set the Indians down in order, and the silence that fell over Jacobs Field was the specific, stunned silence of a city that had now lost three World Series in four years — the window that seemed so wide in 1995 closing a little further.
