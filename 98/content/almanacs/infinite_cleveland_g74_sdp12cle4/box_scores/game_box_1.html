World Series to San Diego 1998
For four hours at Jacobs Field the game had been a proper October argument, Cleveland and San Diego trading the lead back and forth through nine regulation innings like two men who refused to let the other have the last word — and then for two more innings nobody said anything at all, the score knotted at four, the bullpens grinding through baserunners and full counts and the kind of silence that forty-six-degree air makes when forty-five thousand people are holding their breath at once. Cleveland's Bartolo Colon and San Diego's Kevin Brown had each labored through six-plus innings of hard, honest work, neither able to put the other team away, and by the time the twelfth inning arrived Paul Assenmacher had been on the mound for Cleveland for three full frames, operating on something between adrenaline and stubbornness.

What Assenmacher got for his trouble was a bases-loaded, two-out, full-count at-bat from Quilvio Veras — the Padres' slight, switch-hitting second baseman who had already doubled to open the game — and Veras, after fouling off pitch after pitch to stay alive, launched a grand slam into the gap in right-center that traveled 397 feet and broke the night in half. Tony Gwynn, who had spent seventy-four of these October evenings finding ways to hurt Cleveland, followed with a solo home run on the very next at-bat, and by the time Wally Joyner cleared the bases with a double into the left-center gap, San Diego had scored eight runs in the inning and the scoreboard read a number that looked like a football result.

There had been real baseball before the flood. Enrique Wilson, Cleveland's twenty-three-year-old third baseman, had driven a Kevin Brown pitch 381 feet into the right-field seats to give the Tribe a 3-2 lead in the fourth, and Chris Gomez's screaming double off Dave Burba in the eighth — 115 miles per hour off the bat, into the left-center gap — had tied it with the kind of swing that suggested the game still had something to say. Trevor Hoffman had come on in the ninth to protect that tie and held it through a walk and a stolen base and a single, his particular brand of inevitability still intact after seventy-four nights of work. But the game outlasted even Hoffman, and what followed was Assenmacher alone against the weight of a lineup that would not stop coming.

In the bottom of the twelfth the game was already over and everyone in the building knew it. Donne Wall needed only to go through the motions and Cleveland needed only to endure them — Lofton singled because Lofton always singled, but Vizquel grounded into a double play and that was enough, the Cleveland faithful rising from their seats in a vast quiet wave, streaming up the ramps and out into the October night while behind them Ramirez struck out to end a game that only the Padres faithful were still there to celebrate.
