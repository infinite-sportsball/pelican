San Diego 1998 Tops Cleveland 1998
Kevin Brown had spent the better part of three hours doing everything the Padres could have asked of him — seven and two-thirds innings of sinkers that kept drilling the bottom of the zone, five hits allowed, a hundred and thirteen pitches thrown into the forty-eight-degree air at Jacobs Field — and still the scoreboard read 1-1 when he left, as though the game itself had decided that no one pitcher's excellence, however thorough, would settle anything in this particular version of the evening. Bartolo Colon, for his part, had been nearly as stubborn, going the full eight regulation innings for Cleveland on a hundred and nine pitches, allowing only Wally Joyner's solo shot in the fifth — a ball that left the bat at a modest eighty-eight miles an hour and still carried three hundred and seventy-eight feet into the right-field seats, which tells you something about the physics of October and the thin cold air above Lake Erie.

Cleveland had answered within half an inning. Enrique Wilson lashed a double into the left-center gap and came around on Einar Diaz's single to center, the throw arriving a step late, and for four more innings the game locked itself into a kind of anxious equilibrium — both starters gone, both bullpens holding, the crowd standing and sitting and standing again through the seventh and eighth and ninth without seeing a single run cross the plate. In the bottom of the eighth, Thome walked and Ramirez worked a nine-pitch at-bat for another free pass, but David Justice lined out to center and forty-five thousand people exhaled at once.

The tenth inning came apart like a poorly sewn seam. Paul Assenmacher walked Steve Finley on four pitches to open, and when Paul Shuey entered and uncorked a wild pitch with runners at first and second, the geometry of the inning shifted irreversibly — Finley at third, Hernandez at second, nobody out. Chris Gomez, who had struck out looking in his only other moment of consequence, drove a double into the right-center gap on a full count that scored both runners, and suddenly the Padres were running through an open door: Gwynn singled home a third run, walks reloaded the bases, and Joyner — who had started all of this five innings earlier with his quiet home run — lined a two-run single through the right side to make it 6-1. In the bottom of the tenth, Hoffman induced a double play on Vizquel's second pitch, and the thing was over. The stadium emptied into the October dark, forty-five thousand dispersing in near-silence, as though they had always known that a game this tightly wound would, in one shuffling of the deck, simply come undone.
