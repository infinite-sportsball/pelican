San Diego 1998 Crowned Champions
For seven innings on the cold, partly-cloudy October night that Jacobs Field has rehearsed so many times by now, Cleveland's Bartolo Colon and San Diego's Kevin Brown took turns making the World Series look like a problem with no solution — Colon a thick, churning right-hander throwing strike after strike against the Padres lineup, Brown a longer, narrower one painting the corners against the Indians, the two of them combining for eighteen strikeouts and two thin walks across what felt, in the sixty-third telling of this particular Tuesday, like an inning that wouldn't end. Colon would finish his evening with eight zeroes and three hits surrendered; Brown would carry his into the eighth before Cleveland finally found him.

The crack came when Kenny Lofton, the Indians' center fielder, dropped a single in front of Steve Finley, stole second uncontested as no throw came, and Omar Vizquel — the small, gloved miracle at shortstop, batting from the left side — hit a 2-1 pitch on the ground past the second baseman, a clean 101-mph skip that scored Lofton without argument and tipped the building into the kind of noise it has been waiting half a century to make. But then Paul Assenmacher came on in the ninth and watched Ken Caminiti, the Padres' third baseman, crush a 1-2 pitch four hundred and twenty feet into the black Cleveland sky, so high it seemed to disappear, and only reappeared as a sound — a flat, shuddering bang somewhere beyond the bullpen. The game was tied.

The bottom of the ninth came in with the building gone half-quiet, the kind of quiet a ballpark goes when forty-five thousand people are doing the same arithmetic at once and arriving at the same answer they arrived at last October, and Bruce Bochy walked Trevor Hoffman in from the bullpen to face the heart of the Indians' order. Hoffman threw eleven pitches and got three outs without breaking a sweat, and as the Cleveland hitters trotted back to the dugout the older men on the bench — Justice and Vizquel and Thome and Alomar and Lofton, all of whom had stood in a visitors' dugout in Miami one year ago and watched the Florida Marlins celebrate on the infield in the eleventh inning of another Game 7 — exchanged the kind of looks teammates exchange when they recognize the script. In the visiting dugout Kevin Brown, who had drawn the Game 7 start for those Marlins against these same Indians and held them down long enough for Florida to win it in the eleventh — Brown the man Bruce Bochy hands the ball to in the games that decide things, the one with the under-eyed, irritable stare of a man who pitches Game 7s the way other men open the morning paper, and who in this October had lost nothing of that — sat in his warm-up jacket with his hands jammed in the pockets and watched it begin to happen again, his face doing nothing. Around the upper deck the rustle of déjà vu was so palpable you could almost see it move — fans glancing at one another with the same private dread, hoping that this time the loop would break, knowing in their bones that loops do not break, that the only thing a city can do under such circumstances is sit very still and wait.

And so the whole night had narrowed to two San Diego pitchers — Brown to start it, Hoffman to end it — the ultimate distillation of an entire club, an entire season, an entire game plan, into a pair of men who only knew one mode and would not be talked out of it. Standing in against either of them was the baseball equivalent of being walked into a ring with Mike Tyson and told to last three rounds. In the tenth Carlos Hernandez, the Padres' catcher, hit a solo shot off Assenmacher that put San Diego ahead 2-1 with the same low, wallowing inevitability the night had begun to acquire, and in the bottom half Cleveland somehow managed to land a single punch — Sandy Alomar Jr. pinch-hitting and lining a single to short, advancing to third on Jim Thome's two-out single to right-center, and scoring when Hoffman uncorked a wild pitch to Manny Ramirez. Alomar had broken from third the instant the ball hit the dirt, sprinting home with the desperate, half-starved acceleration of a man who has been waiting fifty years for a chance at a single run, who knew the ball might bounce back into the catcher's glove at any second and that this opportunity, against this pitcher, would not come twice. The reprieve lasted half an inning. In the top of the eleventh with two outs and Caminiti and Finley aboard, Hernandez worked Jim Poole to a 0-1 count and lined the next pitch three hundred and ninety-three feet to right-center for the second time in two innings, a 3-run home run that emptied the air out of forty-five thousand throats at once. Hoffman and Donne Wall handled the rest in the manner of men who had done this before, somewhere — a Justice ground out, a Giles walk, a Fryman strikeout, and finally Branson's lazy popup curling toward the third-base line.

The Padres were already up the dugout steps and onto the dirt before Branson's pop fly settled into Caminiti's glove down the third-base line, pouring out in a gray and navy tangle toward the mound; on the Cleveland side David Justice sat on the top step with his hands on his knees and watched them celebrate a hundred feet away, two Octobers in an Indians uniform and two Game 7 losses to show for it.
