San Diego 1998 Triumphs in World Series
The rain at Jacobs Field had thinned to a kind of steady October drizzle by the time the lineup cards were exchanged for the forty-third time, the sort of weather that lacquers the infield grass and slows the batter's breath into visible clouds. The Padres' starter Kevin Brown had given the Indians one run in the third — Kenny Lofton walking, stealing second, scoring on a Jim Thome single through the right side — and from there he settled into the long, deliberate work that has always defined him on his best nights, his sinker bearing in on the hands of left-handers, his slider arriving late and unkind.

Cleveland's Bartolo Colon, for his part, was every bit Brown's equal through five, until the fifth turned on him by way of an error, a base on balls, and a Tony Gwynn line drive to right that scored Carlos Hernandez and would have left it at 2-1 except that Manny Ramirez's throw home sailed past the catcher and Quilvio Veras kept on running. Two unearned runs, a four-pitch ball game, and Colon walked off the mound at the end of the sixth having allowed nothing he could properly call his own. The Indians answered with the hardest-hit ball of the night — Omar Vizquel turning on an 0-2 fastball and lining it 113 miles an hour off the right-center wall for a leadoff triple — and Thome's second RBI single tied it at two. For an inning and a half the duel resumed.

It cracked open in the top of the eighth, against the Cleveland left-hander Jim Poole. Gwynn led off with a double to right, Greg Vaughn was given an intentional pass, and a Ken Caminiti grounder back to the mound that Poole misplayed loaded the bases with nobody out. Jim Leyritz then grounded a 2-1 pitch to the second baseman Jeff Branson, who started a 4-2-3 double play that retired Gwynn at the plate and Leyritz at first — a play that, in some other version of this evening, might have been enough to hold the line. But Wally Joyner, the Padres' first baseman, had been working the count. He fouled off the 2-1, fouled off two more at 2-2, hovering and persistent like an overfed fly slowly buzzing in Poole's face, and on the seventh pitch laced a line drive into right-center at 110 miles an hour that scored Vaughn from third and brought Caminiti home from second when Lofton's throw arrived a half-step late. 4-2, San Diego.

Trevor Hoffman, who pitches in the manner of someone who has read your future and finds it unflattering, came on to record the last four outs. He struck out Manny Ramirez and David Justice in the eighth, then turned the ninth into a kind of chamber piece — Brian Giles grounded out, Enrique Wilson froze on a 3-2 changeup, and Jeff Branson went down swinging. The crowd that had stood for an hour in cold rain stayed standing, motionless, while Hoffman walked off the mound with the small private smile of a man closing a door he has closed many times before, and out at first base Wally Joyner stood with his glove on his hip and his head tilted slightly back, looking at nothing in particular, the bothersome houseguest who had quietly broken Cleveland's heart when everyone least expected.
