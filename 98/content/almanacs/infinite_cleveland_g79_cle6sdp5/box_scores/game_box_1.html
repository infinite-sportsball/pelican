Indians Take Title
Kevin Brown struck out ten Cleveland batters in five innings of work and still lost, which tells you something about the particular geometry of this October evening — a night when the ball carried in the forty-four-degree air as though the cold had made it angry, when both lineups treated the strike zone as a personal insult, and when twenty-five hits rattled around Jacobs Field like a box of silverware dropped down a staircase. The Padres' starter was, by the numbers, devastating: ten strikeouts against a single walk, the kind of line that belongs to a man who ought to be spraying champagne. But Cleveland's Omar Vizquel kept lining doubles into the right-field corner — two of them, first pitch and first pitch, as if he'd memorized the script — and the Indians' first baseman Jim Thome, who would finish 3-for-5 with four RBI, drove a 3-2 pitch 352 feet into the seats in the first inning with Vizquel already standing on third, having stolen it moments before. By the time Thome doubled home another run in the fourth, Brown had allowed ten hits in five innings, and the Padres trailed 5-0, and the evening appeared to be a formality.

It was not a formality. San Diego committed three errors and still nearly won the game, which is the kind of sentence that only makes sense in October. Ken Caminiti's two-run homer to left in the sixth — a full-count, eight-pitch battle against Bartolo Colon that ended 377 feet away — cut the lead to 5-3, and when Chris Gomez singled home Wally Joyner in the seventh, it was 5-4, and Colon's hundred-pitch evening had begun to curdle. Colon gutted through seven innings, ten hits, no walks, and the particular exhaustion of a man who has been hit hard but not quite hard enough, while behind him the Cleveland defense played errorless ball and turned two double plays, as though someone in the dugout had quietly decided that the pitching would wobble but the leather would not.

The insurance run came in the eighth on an infield single by Einar Diaz — a ground ball hit 83 miles per hour that had no business scoring anybody, except that Enrique Wilson had singled and taken second on a Gwynn error, tagged to third on a fly ball, and was running on contact. It made the score 6-4 and felt, for a moment, like enough. Then Joyner led off the ninth against closer Paul Assenmacher and hit a 3-1 pitch 375 feet into the right-field seats, and the margin was one, and forty-five thousand people who had been composing their celebrations suddenly remembered what sport they were watching. Assenmacher struck out Finley, struck out Hernandez, and when Gomez lifted a fly ball to right to end it, Ramirez squeezed it in his glove and turned toward the infield already running, and Thome — the World Series MVP, who had put the first two runs on the board and driven in four — was the first man to reach him, lifting the right fielder off his feet at the edge of the grass.
