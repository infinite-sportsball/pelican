World Series to San Diego 1998
Cleveland's Bartolo Colon pitched eight innings of two-hit ball in this nineteenth rendering of the evening — eight innings in which the Padres managed nothing beyond a Quilvio Veras groundball that found Enrique Wilson's glove and slipped past it, a sacrifice fly, and a whole lot of silence — and it was not enough. It was not enough because baseball has a ninth inning, and because Paul Assenmacher, the left-hander summoned to protect a 4-1 lead, discovered in the cruelest possible sequence what it means to face a lineup that has spent eight innings storing up its fury.

Veras led off the ninth with a double to right-center on a 2-2 count, and after Gwynn grounded out to move him to third, Greg Vaughn lined a single through the left side to make it 4-2. Then Ken Caminiti, the Padres' third baseman, who had struck out twice against Colon and done nothing all night to suggest what was coming, turned on a 3-1 fastball and drove it 388 feet into the left-center field seats — a two-run homer that tied the game at four and turned Jacobs Field into a place where 45,225 people stood in the 42-degree air and tried to remember how to breathe. Jim Leyritz, the next batter, worked the count to 2-2, fouled off two more pitches as if to extend the agony, and then hit a line drive 439 feet to right-center. The ball cleared everything. San Diego 5, Cleveland 4, and the Padres' dugout emptied onto a field that did not belong to them.

Colon had been magnificent — the kind of start that should have ended a fifty-year drought, that should have sent Cleveland into the streets weeping and finally unburdened of 1948. He had earned it. Cleveland had built its lead the hard way: Vizquel's triple and Thome's single in the third, a sacrifice fly in the fourth, Wilson's two-run single in the fifth that scored both Thome and Justice. Four runs against Kevin Brown, who had thrown 115 pitches trying to hold them off. And then Assenmacher recorded one out and gave up four hits and two home runs, and everything Colon had built — every strikeout, every ground ball, every inning of two-hit silence — was rubble. Trevor Hoffman came in to face Cleveland in the bottom of the ninth, and Sandy Alomar Jr. singled to lead off, and for a moment the ballpark gathered itself one last time. But Lofton grounded into a fielder's choice, Vizquel grounded into another, and Thome — who had driven in the first run, who had singled twice, who stood at the plate representing everything Cleveland had left — worked the count full, fouled off two more, and swung through a pitch he never saw. The stadium did not empty so much as deflate, 45,225 people exhaling at once into the October cold, the curse intact, the wait extending into some other year that felt, at that moment, like it might never come.
