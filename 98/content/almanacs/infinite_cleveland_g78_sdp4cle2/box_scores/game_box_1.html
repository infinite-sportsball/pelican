San Diego 1998 Tops Cleveland 1998
For six innings on a cold, rain-spattered Tuesday in Cleveland, the Padres' Kevin Brown and the Indians' Bartolo Colon traded zeroes with the grim professionalism of two men who understood that everything — a title, a drought, a season's worth of accumulated nerve — hung on who blinked first. Brown was the sharper of the two, working at the corners with a sinker that produced eleven ground-ball outs through his first five innings, but Colon matched him with enough raw velocity to keep the San Diego lineup off balance, and the scoreboard stood frozen through five, the crowd at Jacobs Field growing louder on every two-strike count as if volume alone could break the spell.

Cleveland cracked it first. In the bottom of the sixth, the Indians' Jeff Branson chopped an infield single into the hole at short, Einar Diaz beat out another to the left side, and after Vizquel coaxed a full-count walk to load the bases with two out, Manny Ramirez lined a 1-2 pitch through the left side to score Branson — and when Vaughn's throw from left field skipped past the cutoff man, Diaz came around too. Two-nothing Cleveland, and for a few minutes the old ballpark felt as if it might lift off its foundations.

But Colon came out for the seventh and the lead came apart around him with the particular cruelty of a team that refuses to hit the ball hard enough to be caught — Gwynn singled, Vaughn reached on a fielder's choice, Caminiti reached on a Branson error at second, and Leyritz lined a single to load the bases. Wally Joyner's first-pitch grounder through the right side scored Vaughn. Steve Finley's infield single past first brought in Caminiti. And Carlos Hernandez's sacrifice fly to right — Ramirez throwing home, not in time — scored Leyritz and gave San Diego a 3-2 lead, three runs assembled from scraps and stubbornness in an inning that felt, in its cumulative small-ball weight, less like a rally than a slow buckling of the Cleveland infield. Gwynn doubled to the right-field wall off Shuey in the eighth, advanced on a wild pitch, and scored on Vaughn's single to make it 4-2 — a final, quiet turn of the knife.

Trevor Hoffman needed seven pitches to end it. Justice went down looking, Giles grounded to first, and Fryman lifted a fly ball to shallow left that Vaughn squeezed in the rain, and there was the Padres' closer standing on the Jacobs Field mound while forty-five thousand people sat in a silence so total you could hear the visiting dugout emptying onto the wet grass, San Diego's players sprinting toward a celebration that belonged to them and to no one in that ballpark, the first championship in franchise history claimed on a night when Cleveland had held the lead and watched it dissolve in the space of a single inning.
