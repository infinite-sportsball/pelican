Indians Win World Series Crown
The rain that fell on Jacobs Field was not a rain anyone wanted, only the cold, steady kind that flecks the brim of a cap and darkens the warning track and forces the grounds crew to keep rolling and re-rolling the tarp like a man fussing with an umbrella in a vestibule. Inside it, Cleveland's twenty-three-year-old starter Bartolo Colon and the Padres' Kevin Brown set about a game so spare and pinched that by the seventh inning the scoreboard still read 1-1 and the only thing accumulating in any quantity was outs. Brown was striking out the Indians in clusters â six of them, eventually â and Colon was answering with five of his own and an infield that turned three double plays behind him, two of them on the right side, off the bat of Vaughn, Leyritz, and Gwynn in succession, as though Cleveland's defense had quietly resolved that since no one would be slugging on this particular Tuesday, no one would be slugged off it either.

Cleveland's first run came in the third on a sequence so small you could miss it between coughs: with two out and the bases empty, the Indians' center fielder Kenny Lofton lined Brown's first pitch into the right-center gap for a double, and Omar Vizquel, in his eleventh telling of this October at-bat, drove a 1-1 grounder down the left-field line that skipped past Caminiti before he could square his hips. Lofton, who had been running on contact, came home easily. San Diego answered an inning later in the manner of teams whose best hitter is the contact man on the bench rather than the slugger in the box: Caminiti singled, Leyritz fouled off three pitches and worked a full count and lined another single up the middle, and Wally Joyner, the Padres' first baseman, smoked a line drive to left-center off Colon that brought Caminiti in standing. From the fifth through the seventh, nothing â or rather, nothing more than the steady accumulation of dampness on the dugout rail, the small dramas of two-out walks that died on first base, and the particular hush of a one-run game where everyone in the park has begun, somewhere around the sixth, to suspect that whatever happens next will be the whole story.

The deadlock cracked in the eighth. Lofton, who had spent the night drawing money out of Brown a coin at a time, ripped a 109-mile-per-hour line drive into the left-field corner for his second double, took third on a Vizquel groundout to short, and then Jim Thome, the Indians' first baseman, settled in for the at-bat that would decide the game. Brown's pitch count had climbed to 107. Thome worked the count full, and on the eighth pitch â a 3-2 fastball with whatever was left in Brown's arm â he stayed back and slapped a grounder through the right side that Joyner could only glance at, his momentum already going the wrong way. Lofton scored standing, and Cleveland held a tenuous 1-run lead, 2-1. Brown got one more out, then walked off the mound at 114 pitches into a wall of boos, the man who had broken Cleveland's heart in Florida one year earlier now broken in turn, in their park, in their rain.

Paul Assenmacher needed six pitches in the ninth. Caminiti slapped a single past Fryman to put the tying run on, Leyritz hit a tailor-made grounder to Branson at second that became 4-6-3 in the time it takes to say the names, and Joyner â the Padres' best hitter on this rainy night â lifted a quiet fly ball to left that David Justice gathered in for the final out, 2-1 Cleveland. What followed at Jacobs Field was the sound a city makes when fifty years of held breath comes out at once, a roar that built faster than the rain could fall through it, the dugout emptying at a sprint, Justice still holding the ball as he ran, the mound buried under the bodies of an entire baseball team. In the center of the pile, somewhere, was Bartolo Colon, the World Series MVP and the kid who had given them six innings of one-run ball, grinning in the rain like he could not believe his own night.
