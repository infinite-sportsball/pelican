It's Over! Cleveland 1998 Defeats San Diego 1998
Through four innings the game stayed tight at 2-2, Kevin Brown of the Padres and Bartolo Colon of the Indians having each given up a careful pair of runs — San Diego scratching across two on a Wally Joyner two-out double in the top of the fourth, Cleveland answering immediately in the bottom half capped by David Justice's RBI single, the night settling, in that 41-degree clarity, into the kind of measured affair Jacobs Field had imagined for itself. Then San Diego's fifth happened, and you could feel the air go out of the place — the small, traveling sense, available to anyone watching closely enough, that something had come loose. Chris Gomez tripled to left-center off Colon to break the tie, Greg Vaughn followed with a 433-foot two-run drive into the right-center bleachers on the tenth pitch of his at-bat, and Ken Caminiti, hardly pausing, sent a solo shot of his own out behind it. Colon was gone before he could record another out, having coughed up seven runs across four-and-a-third innings, the Padres ahead 7-2, the crowd quieted into that particular, accumulated October stillness that Cleveland has spent fifty years rehearsing.

What followed was the bottom of the seventh, into which Cleveland sent eleven men. Brown was long gone — six steady innings, gone in favor of the bullpen — and the parade of relievers slowly, then suddenly, came undone. With runners on second and third against Randy Myers, the Indians' Jim Thome lined the first pitch he saw, a 105-mile-per-hour bullet to left-center that scored Einar Diaz from third and Kenny Lofton all the way from second on a throw home, Thome himself sliding into second as the relay sailed past, the game tied at seven and the ballpark loud in a way it had not been loud all evening. Two outs later, with runners at the corners, Enrique Wilson, the Cleveland infielder who had only made the start because the matchup demanded it, fell behind 0-2 to Myers and then drove the next pitch on a low rope into the left-center gap for a two-run double, scoring Thome and giving Cleveland an 8-7 lead it had not held at any prior moment of the night.

Paul Assenmacher worked the ninth like a man who had been waiting his whole career for that one half-inning: a Veras flyout, a Gwynn double that quickened every pulse in the ballpark, then Vaughn looking at strike three, and finally Caminiti tapping a soft grounder back up the line. Assenmacher fielded it himself and flipped to Thome at first, and before the glove had quite closed Jacobs Field came apart in a single sustained roar, fifty Octobers of accumulated waiting unspooling at once, the Indians sprinting from the dugout in a churning blue wave to swallow the mound, Wilson in the middle of it being mobbed by men three inches taller than he was, the cold suddenly unaccountable, the noise reaching that particular pitch a city makes when something it has stopped believing in arrives anyway.
