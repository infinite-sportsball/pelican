Padres Beat Indians in World Series
Rain had been falling on Jacobs Field since the late afternoon — a thin, persistent forty-seven-degree drizzle that beaded on the tarp edges and softened the lights into smeary halos — and by the time Padres right-hander Kevin Brown finished his warmups in the top of the first, the 45,225 huddled in their wet parkas might already have sensed, in the way that crowds sometimes know a thing before the scoreboard does, that this particular evening was going to belong to him. Brown worked with the unsmiling economy of a man who had decided the weather was someone else's problem; he threw two-seamers that bored in on the hands of Cleveland's right-handers and a slider that died at the knees, and when Bartolo Colon, the Indians' young starter, took the hill in the top of the second the contrast was already present in the air, the way one pitcher's stuff can simply look heavier than the other's under the same lights.

San Diego scratched first in the third on a Jim Leyritz sacrifice fly and added another in the top of the fifth when Tony Gwynn Sr., the Padres' right fielder and lifelong San Diego fixture, slapped an 0-1 pitch through the left side to score Quilvio Veras from second. By that point Brown had also retired the first twelve Cleveland hitters in order, the no-hitter quietly accumulating like a draft under a door, and when David Justice led off the bottom of the fifth and grounded a single up the middle on the second pitch he saw, the polite, half-rueful applause that came down out of Jacobs Field was less for Justice than for the small mercy of having been spared the additional indignity of a no-hit Game 7. The score was only 2-0, technically a recoverable margin in late October, but the way Brown was working it might as well have been twenty: Cleveland's hitters were swinging like men trying to lift their bats out of wet cement, the lone hit of the night already in the books and no second one anywhere in sight. The sixth inning was the one in which the bottom went out altogether. Colon, who would finish with five and two-thirds innings, seven hits, six runs, and the bewildered look of a man trying to grip a baseball that kept turning to soap, gave up a leadoff double to Steve Finley and then watched Padres shortstop Chris Gomez line a 105-mph two-out double to left for the third run; before the inning was over Mike Hargrove had walked to the mound twice, Jim Poole had taken Colon's place and given up a two-run double to Ken Caminiti, and six Padres had scored, and the rain seemed to come down a little harder, a little colder, with each run.

The seventh and eighth innings unfolded as a slow public dismantling — Quilvio Veras's two-run line-drive homer off Jim Poole in the seventh that traveled three hundred and seventy-four feet into the left-field seats, three more runs in the eighth, the scoreboard ticking upward in that awful, accumulating way that scoreboards do when there is no answer coming. Brown, meanwhile, was finishing what he started: nine innings, one hit, two walks, four strikeouts, a complete-game one-hitter in a World Series Game 7 in the seventieth telling of this particular October Tuesday, and you could see him on the mound in the ninth working faster, almost impatient now, as though even he wanted the thing to be over.

When Omar Vizquel lifted a 3-2 fly to left for the final out, the Padres erupted from their dugout and mobbed Brown on the mound while the Cleveland bench sat with their caps still on, gloves in their laps, staring out at the celebration through the rain that was somehow still falling.
