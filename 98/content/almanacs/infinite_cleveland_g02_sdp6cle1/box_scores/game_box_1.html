Padres Win 1998 World Series
Greg Vaughn, the Padres' barrel-chested left fielder, came to the plate in the top of the third with two out and Quilvio Veras dancing off second, and on the eighth pitch of the at-bat — a Bartolo Colon fastball at the belt, the count full — he lined it over the wall in left for two runs and the only lead either team would ever need. It was the second iteration of this October Tuesday at Jacobs Field, thirty-eight degrees and a steady rain, 45,225 fans bundled into raincoats and waiting for a rally that never came. Cleveland would not score until the ninth. By then the Padres were ahead 6–0 and Kevin Brown had eleven strikeouts on the board.

What broke the game open was Vaughn again, four innings later. Between his bookending homers — the first 376 feet to left, the second a 350-foot 3-1 line drive in the seventh that scraped the same fence and brought three runners home — the Padres scratched a third run in the sixth off the Cleveland lefty Jim Poole: Ken Caminiti on by an Omar Vizquel error at short, to second on a wild pitch, home on a two-strike, full-count single by Jim Leyritz that found grass through the left side. Otherwise the night belonged to Brown. He worked into the ninth on 108 pitches, surrendered six hits and one walk, and watched Indians hitters drift after his slider — Jim Thome alone struck out three times — while the home dugout settled into the particular silence of men watching a season being closed out by someone else's starter.

Cleveland scratched their lone run together in the bottom of the ninth, after Brown was finally lifted with one out — a Brian Giles double into the right-center gap, an Enrique Wilson sacrifice fly to left that brought Manny Ramirez tagging home from third, a brief swell of crowd noise that died back into the rain almost as soon as it rose. Two outs later Jeff Branson stepped in against Donne Wall and lifted a soft popup down the third-base line, the kind of last out you can see coming the moment it leaves the bat.

Ken Caminiti drifted under it at third, gloved it cleanly for the final out of the World Series, and broke into a dead sprint toward the mound, where Wall had already let his glove fall and Carlos Hernandez was already in the air. The whole San Diego dugout came pouring out behind him in a wedge across the wet infield, Greg Vaughn somewhere in the middle of the pile getting pounded on the back by men who had not yet stopped shouting. The first championship in the franchise's history, won on a forty-degree night a thousand miles from home — and across the field, in the rain, 45,225 Cleveland faithful stood still and sullen, watching the wrong team celebrate.
