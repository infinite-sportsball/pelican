In the seventy-fifth version of this particular October Tuesday, the Padres' Kevin Brown pitched as if the cold couldn't find him — seven full innings at Jacobs Field, 114 pitches, bending a Cleveland lineup that kept reaching base but could never quite land the blow that mattered. Brown gave up eight hits and walked four, which on paper suggests a man under siege, but the Indians stranded nine runners in those seven innings, and there is a kind of pitching dominance that lives not in zeroes but in the way a man escapes the noose over and over, leaving the rope swinging empty behind him.

The game had been tied at two since the bottom of the fourth, when Cleveland's Jeff Branson — a utility infielder playing the biggest game of his life — laced a double into the left-center gap at 112 miles per hour off the bat, scoring David Justice and Brian Giles in one stroke, erasing a deficit that had felt, for four half-innings, like it might be enough. Three innings of zeroes followed, the kind of tense, airless baseball that makes forty-four-degree weather feel ten degrees colder, and for a while it seemed as though the night might belong to whichever side flinched last.

San Diego's Carlos Hernandez, the Padres' catcher, answered in the top of the seventh. Steve Finley led off with a double to deep right-center, and on a 1-1 pitch Hernandez drove a ball 382 feet to left field, a two-run homer that broke the tie and, as it turned out, broke the game. Cleveland's David Justice pulled them within one in the bottom of the inning, singling home Kenny Lofton on a 2-2 groundball past first, and for a moment the old ballpark shook with the sound of a crowd that still believed. But the eighth belonged entirely to San Diego — three more runs off reliever Paul Shuey, the damage done by Wally Joyner's single, Finley's second double of the night, and Hernandez's sac fly, each hit arriving with the businesslike precision of men who sensed the door was open and intended to walk through it before anyone changed the lock.

Randy Myers needed only thirteen pitches to close it. Jim Thome, who had doubled in the fifth and done nothing else all night, went down swinging on three pitches in his final at-bat, and when Manny Ramirez grounded softly to third to end it, the Padres' Ken Caminiti squeezed the throw at first and held the ball for just a moment longer than necessary — a man letting the weight of what had just happened settle into his hands while his teammates poured from the visiting dugout behind him.
