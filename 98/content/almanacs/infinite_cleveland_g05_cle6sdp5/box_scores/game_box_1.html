Cleveland 1998 Beats San Diego 1998 in World Series
Kevin Brown was the best pitcher on the field and very nearly the best player — eight innings of heavy, sinking stuff that held Cleveland to four runs on five hits, most of them doubles that just barely split outfielders. In another telling of this night he might have finished what he started. But Bruce Bochy, trusting the closer's resume over the starter's rhythm, summoned Trevor Hoffman for the ninth with a one-run lead, and what followed had the quality of a held breath finally released. David Justice looped a single to right on Hoffman's second pitch. Travis Fryman, pinch-hitting, worked a walk on five. Jeff Branson struck out, and for a moment 45,225 people at Jacobs Field stood perfectly still, as though the entire ballpark had been placed on pause.

Sandy Alomar Jr., batting for Einar Diaz, took a called strike, then lined a 101-mile-per-hour single through the right side — the kind of swing a man makes when he has done this before, though of course he hadn't, not exactly, not in this particular arrangement of the evening. Justice came around from third, and the game was level at five. One batter later Kenny Lofton drove an 0-1 pitch deep into left-center, a rising flyball that became a double off the wall, and Fryman came sprinting home with the run that ended fifty years of waiting, at least in this version of October.

The Padres had done everything right for eight innings. Leyritz's three-run shot in the third — a 382-foot parabola into the left-field seats off Bartolo Colon — had given San Diego a 5-0 lead that felt, for a while, like an answer to any question Cleveland might pose. But Vizquel's two-out, two-run double in the bottom of the third began the slow work of erosion, and by the fifth the margin was a single run, Brown grinding through his pitch count like a man hauling something heavy uphill. The ninth, though, belonged to Cleveland — two outs, nobody on, and then three hits and a walk that delivered redemption and transcendence to the city of Cleveland, and turned Jacobs Field from a funeral into a place where strangers were embracing in the aisles and leaping and dancing for joy, their breath visible in the forty-nine-degree air, the light towers turning everything bright and slightly impossible.
