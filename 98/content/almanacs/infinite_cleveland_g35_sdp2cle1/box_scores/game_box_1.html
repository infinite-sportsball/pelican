San Diego 1998 Padres Are Champs
There are nights when the rain arrives early and stays, slicking the brim of every cap and lending the infield dirt a dark, mineral gloss, and on such nights the game contracts around the pitching — the outfield grass going unused, the bat rack a place of quiet disappointment. This was one of those nights, and it belonged almost entirely to the Padres' Kevin Brown, who worked eight innings of sparse, ground-hugging efficiency into the forty-one-degree drizzle, surrendering five hits and one run while striking out seven, his sinker boring in on Cleveland's left-handed hitters as though it had memorized every one of their swings from some prior encounter.

Cleveland had taken the lead in the second on a double by the Indians' third baseman Enrique Wilson, who lined one into the left-center gap to score Brian Giles from first — the kind of early run that in a different telling might have been the seed of a rout. But Brown settled immediately, and meanwhile the Padres' left fielder Greg Vaughn opened the fourth with a double off the right-center wall, Ken Caminiti followed with another double that split left and center to tie it, and Wally Joyner's hard single through the right side scored Caminiti to make it 2-1. Cleveland's starter Bartolo Colon, who was nearly Brown's equal on the night — seven innings, six strikeouts, just those two runs — loaded the bases in the bottom of the fourth but escaped without further damage, and the score held there for five long, rain-soaked innings in which Brown seemed to throw harder as the night wore on.

When Trevor Hoffman came in for the ninth, the crowd of 45,225 stirred briefly: David Justice worked a full-count walk, and the tying run stood at first with one out. But Hoffman struck out Giles on a check-swing and then got the pinch-hitter Travis Fryman to roll a first-pitch grounder to short — Chris Gomez to Quilvio Veras to Joyner, the prettiest kind of ending if you were wearing the wrong uniform, a 6-4-3 double play that traveled around the horn in under three seconds and left Jacobs Field as quiet as the rain.
