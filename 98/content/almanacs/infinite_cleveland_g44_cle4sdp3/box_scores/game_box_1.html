Indians Win 1998 World Series
It is forty-two degrees in Cleveland on this October Tuesday, the kind of late-autumn cold that makes a ballpark feel both larger and more brittle than it does in July, and for six innings the game seems organized around the proposition that nobody is going to score very much at all. Kevin Brown, the Padres' right-hander, is in one of those moods where the slider arrives at the knees and disappears; he will strike out ten Indians before the night is done. Bartolo Colon, working for Cleveland, matches him pitch for pitch, the only blemish a third-inning San Diego rally that yields a single run on a Ken Caminiti infield squib — a chopper that dies in the grass and stands, briefly, as the entire offense of the evening.

The game cracks open in the bottom of the sixth on a swing nobody in the building expects. Jeff Branson, the Indians' second baseman, takes Brown's first strike, then sends the second one — a ball not catastrophically mislocated, only ordinarily so — three hundred and ninety-seven feet to right-center, where it clears the wall with the unhurried trajectory of a thing that has decided to be a home run. Two batters later the Indians' shortstop Omar Vizquel doubles Einar Diaz home from second, and Cleveland, which has been chasing Brown's slider all night, leads 2-1.

The Padres claw the run back in the seventh against Cleveland reliever Paul Shuey on a Tony Gwynn double to the corner — Gwynn, the Padres' right fielder, will finish three-for-five, collecting hits like a man pulling coins from a fountain — and a Greg Vaughn fly ball to deep center that scores him from third. The eighth is harder still: with two out and runners on the corners, Chris Gomez, the Padres' shortstop, lines a single off Cleveland lefty Jim Poole to plate Jim Leyritz, and San Diego leads 3-2 going to the bottom half.

In the bottom of the eighth Vizquel works a four-pitch walk off Brown, and Trevor Hoffman comes out of the San Diego bullpen. Manny Ramirez, the Indians' right fielder, lines the 2-0 pitch to left at a hundred and six miles an hour, and Vaughn — who has played a long evening of left field — sails his throw past the cutoff; Vizquel scores the tying run on the error and Ramirez takes second. Two outs later David Justice, the Indians' left fielder, fights off a 2-2 fastball and lifts it to short left, where a visibly shaken Vaughn fields it and throws home, just a beat behind Ramirez sliding in headfirst and touching the plate for a 4-3 lead and a thick, beautiful stream of brown Jacobs Field dirt down the front of his jersey. Paul Assenmacher closes the ninth in eight pitches — Caminiti looking at strike three to end it — and Jacobs Field comes apart at the seams.

Forty-five thousand people are on their feet and screaming, an entire section of men with their shirts off, steaming and screaming and ecstatic, a complete contrast to Bartolo Colon shivering under a fur coat and blowing hard on his hands to keep them warm. The screaming Cleveland mob that has long since finished their last beers from the seventh inning stretch gets drunk on a fifty-year scotch — a World Series ring for Cleveland. The clear sky chooses this moment to spit a thin, improbable sprinkle of rain across the infield as the Indians spill out of their dugout and into one another's arms at the mound, and Bartolo Colon, the Player of the Game, his six innings of one-run baseball already ledgered into the long memory of a city that has been keeping count, gets there last and gets buried first, laughing, the rain on his face, a champion at last.
