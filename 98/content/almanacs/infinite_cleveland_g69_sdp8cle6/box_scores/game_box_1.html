San Diego 1998 Padres Capture World Series
Padres right-hander Kevin Brown walked out into a 47-degree Cleveland night that would not, in the end, behave the way either starting pitcher imagined it might, and for the first six innings of the sixty-ninth telling of this October Tuesday he labored through traffic — eight hits, five runs, three walks, the kind of evening where a pitcher survives mostly because the lineup keeps deciding it isn't quite ready to bury him. His counterpart, Cleveland's Bartolo Colon, gave it back in lopsided installments: a four-run second cracked open by a Wally Joyner line-drive double into right-center that put runners at second and third, then six and a third gritted-out frames that ended on the worst possible pitch of his night.

The game arrived at the seventh tied to no particular shape. Cleveland had grabbed a one-run lead in the fifth on a David Justice single and a Enrique Wilson double, and 45,225 people in the stands had begun to allow themselves the small private arithmetic of a one-run lead in October — six outs to go, then nine, then the long unspooling of fifty years. Then Quilvio Veras, the Padres' second baseman, walked and stole second, and Tony Gwynn Sr., still squaring up the baseball at thirty-eight as if he had figured out something the rest of us hadn't, lined a 1-1 pitch from Colon 361 feet into the right-field seats. Two batters later, after Greg Vaughn had doubled off Cleveland reliever Jim Poole, Leyritz turned on the first pitch he saw and drove it 370 feet the other way into the left-field seats, and the ballpark went into that particular kind of quiet that forty-five thousand people make when they have just watched a three-run cushion appear on the wrong side of the scoreboard.

Cleveland did not go meekly. Brian Giles, the Indians' young left fielder, doubled home a run in the bottom of the seventh to cut it to 8-6, and the place leaned forward again, and stayed leaned forward through a quiet eighth. Then the bullpen door swung open and out came Trevor Hoffman, and the air went out of Jacobs Field the way air goes out of a room when the doctor walks in and his face has not arranged itself into anything good. Forty-five thousand people sat down at once. The cold seemed to have been poured into the place. Hoffman finished his warm-up tosses, set himself on the rubber, and Game 7 began to feel, all at once, like a long, empty hallway with a brick wall at the end of it.

Omar Vizquel flied to left on the third pitch of the inning, a hard line drive that Greg Vaughn squared up under and held. Then Jim Thome stepped in and did the unimaginable: he watched Hoffman throw four straight balls. Not a foul, not a swing, not a single contested pitch — just Thome standing in the box with his bat on his shoulder and refusing to give Hoffman anything to work with, until ball four sent him jogging up the line and the building lifted itself off its seats again with the dim memory of what hope used to feel like. Manny Ramirez followed, fouled a pitch off, took a called strike, fouled off another, took a ball, and finally swung through a 1-2 pitch for strike three. Two outs.

David Justice walked to the plate with the season on his bat, the man who had opened the night nine innings earlier with a 4-6 fielder's choice in the bottom of the first that had pushed Kenny Lofton across with the game's first run. He had bookended the night already in his head. Hoffman threw one pitch — one — and Justice lifted a routine flyball to right, a soft 83-mile-per-hour can of corn that Tony Gwynn settled under in front of his own glove for the third out and the World Series. Justice stood at home plate for half a second to confirm what his eyes had told him, and then he walked back toward the Cleveland dugout the way he had walked back toward a Cleveland dugout one October ago in Miami — head down, brim of his cap pulled low — still without a ring, still bookending the wrong end of the night, while around him the Padres poured out toward the mound and Hoffman held the ball aloft and Leyritz sprinted in from behind the plate and the Cleveland faithful rose at once and started for the exits, leaving only the small scattered pockets of Padres fans who had made the trip to watch their team celebrate on Jacobs Field grass.
