San Diego 1998 Padres Capture World Series
The Padres' designated hitter, Jim Leyritz, had already been in the middle of the night's first strange sequence — a second-inning single up the middle off Bartolo Colon, then Wally Joyner lining a double into right-center behind him, Leyritz rounding third and coming home ahead of the throw while Joyner, seeing the ball leaving the outfielder's hand toward the plate, took third standing up. It was the sort of play that reads on the scoreboard as one run and one double and hides, in that shorthand, the small collapse of a defense that had chosen the wrong base. By the time the second inning was over — a wild pitch here, an error there — San Diego had put up a four-spot and Colon was pitching from behind in a Game 7 he had walked into with fifty years of Cleveland waiting on his right arm.

The night pretended, for a while, to belong to Cleveland. The Indians answered with two in the bottom of the second on an Einar Diaz single and a Kenny Lofton double, took the lead in the fifth on Enrique Wilson's RBI double and a David Justice single, and Jacobs Field had begun to do the private math of a one-run October evening — twelve outs, and then the long unspooling of nineteen forty-eight. Kevin Brown, the Padres' starter, had been working through traffic all night and finding, somehow, the pitch he needed; Colon had settled into a kind of grim rhythm. Then the seventh came apart. Quilvio Veras walked and stole second. Tony Gwynn, still doing at thirty-eight what he had been doing at twenty-eight, lined a 1-1 pitch three hundred and sixty-one feet into the right-field seats — a two-run home run pulled the way Gwynn almost never pulls. Two batters later, after Greg Vaughn had doubled off Cleveland reliever Jim Poole, Leyritz stepped in and, on the first pitch he saw, drove it the other way — a right-hander going the opposite way three hundred and seventy feet into the left-field bleachers, the mirror image of Gwynn's ball, one blast to each corner of the outfield in the same inning off two different Cleveland pitchers.

Cleveland fought. Brian Giles doubled home a run in the bottom of the seventh to cut it to 8-6, and the eighth was quiet, and then the bullpen door in right-center opened and Trevor Hoffman came in for the ninth. The building leaned forward one more time. Vizquel flied to left on a 2-0 pitch. Thome, who had spent the entire evening being carefully avoided, watched four balls go by and jogged to first without offering at a pitch. Ramirez fouled off two and swung through a 1-2 breaking ball for the second out. David Justice, who had walked to the plate with the season on his bat and some private accounting of one October ago in Miami surely in his head, saw one pitch and lifted a soft flyball to right, an 83-mile-per-hour can of corn that Gwynn drifted under and gathered in with the small, practiced economy of a man who has caught a great many baseballs in his life.

Leyritz, as designated hitter, had no glove; he just stood and watched the final three outs from the top step of the dugout. When the ball settled into Gwynn's mitt in shallow right he was already over the rail, sprinting across the Jacobs Field grass toward the pile forming at the mound — the man who had scored the tying run in the second and hit the ball that broke the game in the seventh. Behind him the Cleveland faithful stood at their seats for a long, quiet moment before they turned, one by one, toward the exits.
