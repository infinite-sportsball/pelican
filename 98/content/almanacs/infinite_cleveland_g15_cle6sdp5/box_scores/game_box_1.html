Cleveland 1998 Indians Win World Series
In this fifteenth retelling, the night very nearly belonged to San Diego. Ken Caminiti opened the second inning by driving a 0-1 pitch from Bartolo Colon 386 feet into the left-field seats — a line drive that left the bat at 109 miles per hour, the sound of it arriving in the upper deck before the crowd had fully registered the swing. Cleveland answered with a sacrifice fly in the bottom half, and for an inning the game breathed at 1-1, a condition that felt, in retrospect, like the last quiet moment of the evening. Then the fourth inning happened. Joyner singled, Finley walked, Hernandez beat out an infield hit to load the bases, and Chris Gomez — the number-nine hitter, the man least likely to rearrange a World Series — lined a first-pitch double into the right-field corner that cleared all three runners and gave San Diego a lead that looked, at 5-1, like something permanent.

Cleveland came back the way a tide comes in — not in one wave but in several, each one reaching a little farther up the sand. A Thome sacrifice fly in the fifth. A wild pitch and a Giles sac fly and an Enrique Wilson double in the sixth, Wilson's liner to right-center leaving the bat at 108 miles per hour, Justice scoring standing up, the deficit down to one. Kevin Brown, who had thrown 107 pitches and two wild pitches and surrendered eight hits while somehow remaining on the mound into the seventh, watched Omar Vizquel line a double into the right-center gap and walked off to whatever solitude a losing pitcher finds in an opponent's visiting clubhouse. Randy Myers inherited Vizquel at second and the crowd's full attention.

In the bottom of the seventh, with two outs and down to a single strike, with Vizquel dancing off second and 45,225 fans rapt in the freezing cold, Manny Ramirez got a pitch he could reach and sent it 374 feet into the left-field seats. The sound that followed was not a cheer so much as a detonation, the noise of a crowd that had watched a four-run deficit dissolve pitch by pitch and had now seen it become a lead. Assenmacher struck out the side around a walk in the ninth, the last out a Steve Finley swing through empty air, and somewhere in the pile forming at the mound was Manny Ramirez, who had been one strike from going home and had instead sent a baseball into the left-field seats and a fifty-year wait into whatever comes after waiting.
