Cleveland 1998 Wins World Series Crown
In the thirty-ninth degree of cold on an October Tuesday, the Padres' Kevin Brown — who in other tellings of this night has buckled knees and silenced the lower deck — found nothing at all, not location, not deception, not even the ordinary cruelty of a major-league sinker. Cleveland's lineup went through him like a turnstile: four runs in the first inning on singles by Manny Ramirez and Enrique Wilson and a David Justice double that cleared the bases with the unhurried authority of a man who had done this before, in some life or another, and knew exactly where the gap would be. By the time Brown walked off in the fourth, having surrendered eleven hits and eight runs in three and two-thirds innings, the outcome was already an administrative matter. The only question was the margin.

San Diego, to their credit, did not go quietly into arithmetic. Dave Burba relieved Bartolo Colon in the sixth and promptly gave back three runs in the seventh — Steve Finley lining a double to left that scored two and briefly made the scoreboard read 8–5, a number that at least resembled a baseball game. But Mark Langston, summoned to restore order in the bottom of the inning, instead presided over its complete dissolution: he walked Wilson, watched Branson and Diaz single, then fell behind Kenny Lofton three balls and two strikes before Lofton drove a grand slam 374 feet into the right-field seats, and the sound that rose from 45,225 people was less a cheer than a kind of civic delirium, the noise of a city that has waited fifty years and cannot quite believe the score is now 12–5.

Cleveland scored eight runs in that seventh inning alone, then five more in the eighth — Thome's triple to deep right, Ramirez's two-run homer, Justice's third double of the evening — as if the lineup had discovered some defect in the simulation's mercy algorithm and meant to exploit it completely. The final score, 21–8, was the kind of number that belongs on a football field, or perhaps in the sort of fever dream a Cleveland fan might have after falling asleep with the radio on. Ramirez finished 4-for-5 with a double, a home run, four runs scored, and four driven in; Lofton matched him line for line. Twenty-four hits in all, against six Padres pitchers. The champagne, when it came, must have seemed redundant.
