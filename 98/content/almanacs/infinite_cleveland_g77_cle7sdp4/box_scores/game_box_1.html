It's Over! Cleveland 1998 Defeats San Diego 1998
Cleveland's starter Bartolo Colon and the Padres' Kevin Brown had spent the better part of six innings locked in a stingy, unyielding contest that made the ballpark go quiet between pitches — Colon surrendering only Ken Caminiti's solo home run in the second, Brown grinding through Cleveland's two-run fourth with the grim efficiency of a man who does not intend to let this get away from him. He very nearly didn't. Brown left after six and a third innings having allowed just two runs, seven strikeouts gleaming in his line like a rebuke to anyone who thought he'd been beaten, and when Tony Gwynn lifted a 1-2 pitch from Paul Shuey over the right-field wall in the seventh — a two-run shot that felt less hit than willed, the ball carrying 366 feet on a night too cold for baseballs to travel that far — Jacobs Field went still the way a theater goes still when the wrong actor walks onstage.

By the time Chris Gomez tripled in an insurance run off Jim Poole in the eighth, stretching San Diego's lead to 4-2, the arithmetic had turned cruel. Cleveland had managed nothing off Randy Myers in the seventh, and the Padres' relief corps had yet to surrender a run all night. But Myers, in one of those collapses that unfold with the slow-motion specificity of a car sliding on ice, never recorded an out in the eighth. Kenny Lofton singled. Omar Vizquel singled. Jim Thome, on the first pitch he saw, lined a double to deep right-center that scored Lofton and sent Vizquel to third, and the sound that rose from the stands was not a cheer so much as a convulsion, a home crowd remembering simultaneously how to breathe.

Manny Ramirez followed with a tying infield single on a 3-1 count, the ball dying on the grass near second base while Vizquel scored standing up. Myers walked David Justice to load the bases and was lifted for Mark Langston, who struck out Brian Giles but then surrendered a go-ahead infield single to Enrique Wilson — a grounder through the left side that barely reached the outfield grass, a hit that in another October might have rolled foul. It did not, and Thome scored to make it 5-4. With two outs, Einar Diaz lined a single to right-center that brought home Ramirez and, when Justice sprinted around from second and beat Gwynn's throw to the plate, the deficit that had been two runs three batters ago was suddenly a 7-4 lead.

The ninth should have been a procession. Instead, Jeff Branson — who had gone hitless in four at-bats, struck out twice, and contributed nothing all evening — set about doing his best to give it all back. Gwynn hit a routine grounder to second and Branson bobbled it, kicked it, and finally picked it up with nowhere to throw. Vaughn struck out looking, and for a moment it seemed the damage was contained. Then Caminiti walked, and Leyritz hit another grounder to second — where Branson let it skip through his legs, loading the bases with one out while the ballpark watched a three-run lead shrink to the width of one more misplay. Dave Burba, who had inherited this circus without having done a single thing wrong, stood on the mound with the patience of a man whose brother-in-law has just backed into his mailbox for the second time. He got Wally Joyner to lift a fly to right, then threw one pitch to Steve Finley, who popped it up toward third, where Travis Fryman squeezed it and vanished beneath a wall of teammates who came pouring off the Cleveland bench as if they'd been shot from a cannon.
