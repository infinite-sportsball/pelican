Cleveland 1998 Indians Are Champs
The rain came down on Jacobs Field in a fine cold mist, the kind that beads on a player's cap brim and makes the light towers look smudged, and for nine and a half innings the Padres looked like the team that was going to walk out of it with a trophy. Tony Gwynn, San Diego's right fielder, had been tormenting the Indians' Bartolo Colon since the first pitch of the night — a double down the right-field line that scored Quilvio Veras, and then, in the seventh, a 414-foot two-run shot that landed somewhere out beyond the bullpens and put the Padres ahead 6-4. The Padres' first baseman Wally Joyner had reached the bleachers in the second on a low line drive that bent toward the seats in left, an opposite-field stroke off a Colon pitch that hung. By the time Kevin Brown departed after six, having allowed only one earned run despite eight hits, San Diego looked ready to do to Cleveland what Florida had done one year and one day earlier in Miami — close the door on a fifty-year wait with another extra-innings October Tuesday, this one in cold rain.

Cleveland kept clawing. Thome, the Indians' first baseman, had answered Joyner almost immediately with a 3-2 home run off Brown in the bottom of the first, a 368-foot drive he watched only briefly before lowering his head. In the second, with two outs and the bases loaded, Einar Diaz hit a medium-speed grounder into the hole between short and third, and Ken Caminiti — the Padres' veteran third baseman, the man you most want that ball to find — charged it, short-hopped his glove, and watched the ball squirt out toward shallow left. Brian Giles scored easily from third. The embarrassing part was Enrique Wilson, who had been running on contact from second base, never breaking stride, rounding third and crossing the plate while Caminiti was still bent at the waist staring at the wet grass where the ball had been. Two runs on a play that should have been the third out, and 3-3. Cleveland scratched ahead 4-3 in the fourth when Diaz singled in Wilson, then surrendered the lead in the sixth on a Greg Vaughn walk that turned, by way of a steal and a Diaz throwing error, into a Padres run on Jim Leyritz's RBI single.

The Indians cut the San Diego lead to 6-5 in the eighth on Kenny Lofton's infield single off Trevor Hoffman, scoring Giles. In the ninth, down to their last out and last strike at various points and with Manny Ramirez standing on third after a Giles single, Travis Fryman — pinch-hitting for Wilson against Donne Wall — punched a 3-1 grounder up the middle that died just past Quilvio Veras's reach. Ramirez scored. The tying run was home, the World Series was going to extra innings, and the Cleveland faithful — forty-five thousand of them shivering in October rain — did not yet trust it; they had stood, one year and one day earlier, watching Edgar Renteria's broken-bat single drop in front of Bonilla in the bottom of the eleventh down in Miami, and they had learned the particular cruelty of Game 7 extra innings. They were not going to celebrate a tie.

In the bottom of the tenth, with one out and the rain coming down harder, Lofton stepped in against Wall on a 1-2 count and turned on the next pitch with sudden, uncoiled violence, a swing he seemed to have been saving his whole career for one specific pitch. The ball climbed into the right-center field night and kept climbing — 399 feet, a flight no outfielder bothered to chase past the warning track — and by the time it landed somewhere in the bleachers Jacobs Field had detonated. Fifty years. Nineteen forty-eight. A half-century of Septembers ending early, and one specific October ending in Florida the year before, all of it gone in the time it took a baseball to travel from a pitcher's hand to a wet patch of Cleveland sky. Lofton rounded the bases at a dead run and disappeared at home plate beneath a pile that grew and grew, the entire Cleveland dugout sprinting out at full speed to bury him, and somewhere at the bottom of all of it was Lofton, who had finally, finally, brought it home.
