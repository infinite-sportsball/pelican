Cleveland 1998 Indians Capture World Series
Rain came down on Jacobs Field in the first inning the way it had been coming down on the parking lots all afternoon, steady and cold and forty-three degrees, and the Padres' right-hander Kevin Brown stood on the mound rubbing the ball dry between pitches and looking generally unbothered. He had walked Jim Thome with one out, given up a soft single to Manny Ramirez, and then watched Cleveland's left fielder David Justice flare a 2-1 pitch into shallow left-center to bring Thome home — a thin, unconvincing run, the kind a starter of Brown's caliber tends to wipe out before the night is over. 1-0, Cleveland, and that, for a long time, was the score.

For five innings, that's where it stayed. Brown punched out eight Indians on his way through six, scattering hits, walking only two, trudging off after a hundred and sixteen pitches with the deficit still a single run — a line that ought to win a ballgame more often than not. What he could not have known, as he handed the ball over, was that the Padres had collected exactly two hits against Bartolo Colon to that point, that the big Cleveland right-hander was working at the knees on every pitch and getting San Diego to roll the ball gently to his infielders, and that Brown's was the only line that night to which the word "duel" could honestly be applied.

The bottom of the seventh came apart slowly for the Padres, the way it does when a tiring starter is asked to give one inning more than he should. Jeff Branson singled to start; Einar Diaz bunted him to second; Kenny Lofton reached when a relay dropped at the bag. With Brown out and Donne Wall on, Omar Vizquel poked an infield single up the middle to score Branson, and then Thome — the Cleveland first baseman, batting left — drove a ball into the left-center gap that was not quite hit hard enough to clear the wall but skipped to it on one bounce, two runs scoring before the relay was in. Justice's sacrifice fly made it four. 5-0, suddenly, and forty-five thousand people who had been keeping their hands inside their jackets brought them out.

What happened in the eighth was the kind of thing this crowd had been waiting fifty years to witness and most of them, in the moment, could not quite believe. With the bases loaded and two outs and Mark Langston in to face him, Thome turned on a 1-2 pitch and sent it 408 feet into the right-field stands — a grand slam that landed in the rain with the muffled finality of a vault door closing, 9-0, and the noise that followed was no longer cheering but something older. Manny Ramirez followed with a solo shot of his own off Randy Myers to extend the lead to double digits, and pour salt into San Diego's fresh wounds. Colon went out for the ninth and finished what he'd started, three hits across eight and two-thirds, and when the last fly ball settled into his right fielder's glove, Thome reached the mound first, hatless and soaked, and was buried in the muddy infield grass by men who had stopped being able to feel the cold. The noise spread outward from the pile the way a wave spreads from a stone — from the infield to the dugouts, from the dugouts up through the lower bowl and into the upper deck, out the gates onto Prospect Street where the people who had not been able to get tickets were already in the road, and from there into living rooms in Tremont and Ohio City and Slavic Village, into kitchens in Lakewood and West Park, into upstairs bedrooms in Shaker Heights where small children had been allowed to stay up. The rain kept coming. The celebrations in the Cleveland night went on, and on, and on.
