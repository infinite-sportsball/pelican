San Diego 1998 Padres Win World Series
For six innings on this particular October Tuesday, the sixty-fourth telling of a night Jacobs Field cannot stop replaying, the 45,225 in their parkas had every reason to think a 2-0 deficit was the kind of hole this team could climb out of — a manageable margin, the kind that breeds a certain northern stubbornness, the sense that one swing or one walk or one ball finding grass might tilt the whole evening sideways. Bartolo Colon, the Cleveland starter, had been hit but not battered: a Carlos Hernandez solo shot in the third, a long carrying flyball that the Padres catcher stood and watched into the left-center seats at 415 feet, and an RBI single by Steve Finley, the Padres center fielder, in the fourth, a line drive through the right side that scored Ken Caminiti from second. Two runs, both off Colon's mistakes, both absorbable. The Indians clawed back the way teams clawed back in the late nineties — slowly, with situational hitting that bordered on penance — Manny Ramirez lifting a sacrifice fly in the sixth to score one, Kenny Lofton lofting another in the seventh to tie it. The light towers buzzed. Two-all. The crowd believed.

What followed was the kind of inning that does not merely change a game but unmoors it from its premises. Jim Poole, summoned to handle the top of the eighth with the lineup turning over, watched Tony Gwynn Sr., the Padres right fielder, square up a 2-2 pitch and lace a leadoff double into the left-field corner, and the rally that followed had the awful steady accumulation of water finding the cracks in a basement. Poole gave way to Paul Shuey; Shuey gave way to Dave Burba; the Padres kept hitting. With two outs and the bases loaded and the score now 5-2, Gwynn came around again and pulled a 1-0 line drive down the left-field line, the kind of double he had been hitting since the Carter administration, and three runs scored, and somewhere in the middle of it the noise in Jacobs Field went out like a fuse. Nine runs. One inning. The board read 11-2 and looked, somehow, both impossible and inevitable.

Kevin Brown, the Padres starter, had given them six and a third of the kind of work — five hits, two runs, six strikeouts on 104 pitches — that on most nights wins quietly and is forgotten. On this night it was simply the platform from which the avalanche came. Randy Myers blew a save and won the game in the same half-inning, a piece of bookkeeping so peculiar that the scorer must have shrugged at his pencil. The bottom of the ninth came and went the way these things do when the verdict is already in: Kenny Lofton, the Cleveland center fielder, who had driven in his team's second run with a sacrifice fly two innings earlier and now stood in with nothing left to play for, grounded weakly to short and watched Einar Diaz get forced at second on the play. He stood for a moment at first base with his hands on his hips and his head down, forty-five thousand in parkas stared past him at the Padres jumping on each other in the middle of the infield, and what the Cleveland faithful among them felt was the cold particular grief of a city that had again been brought to the lip of the thing and again, in this telling, been told no.
