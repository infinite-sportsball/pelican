Cleveland 1998 Takes Title
By the time Kevin Brown walked off the Jacobs Field mound in the third inning — eight hits behind him, six runs on the board, his evening over at sixty-odd pitches — the Cleveland faithful had already begun to grasp what kind of October Tuesday this would be. All evening, the ball kept finding green where the Padres weren't. The ovation as the San Diego ace trudged off was not for him; it was the sound of a city beginning, very carefully, to allow itself to believe.

The third inning unspooled the way a long inning does when nothing goes right for the visitors — hit after hit, each one landing where no glove waited. Enrique Wilson lined a 2-2 pitch into left for a double; Einar Diaz, the Indians catcher, slapped a grounder through the left side to score him; Kenny Lofton singled to right; Omar Vizquel ran the count to 2-0 and lined a single up the middle for another run; Manny Ramirez squibbed a hit down the third-base line that the Cleveland dugout had identified before it stopped rolling. David Justice, the Indians left fielder, then drove a 1-1 pitch into the right-center gap for a two-run double — Tony Gwynn declined the throw home because there was nothing to throw at — and Wilson came back to the plate before the inning ended and doubled in another. Six runs, seven hits, the deck cutting itself in real time.

Bartolo Colon nursed the cushion into the sixth and very nearly gave it back. Manny Ramirez had stretched the margin in the fifth, a 2-1 pitch from reliever Donne Wall driven 397 feet into the seats in left-center, but Padres catcher Carlos Hernandez answered in the sixth with a three-run home run, a 2-1 pitch off Colon that came down 406 feet later in the same general acre Ramirez had reached. A low silence settled over the ballpark. Jim Poole came on and caught Chris Gomez swinging, the building exhaled, and a two-run lead was the only thing left standing between this ballclub and 1948 going away forever.

Poole and Paul Assenmacher walked the last nine men back to the dugout without a hit between them. Chris Gomez was the twenty-seventh, and when his bat went through the 2-2 pitch and found nothing but Cleveland air, Jacobs Field came apart — fifty years of waiting let loose at once, a sound that did not so much rise as detonate. Ramirez was already running. He came in from right field at a dead sprint, glove flung somewhere behind him in the grass, and reached the mound at the same moment Einar Diaz did, the two of them hitting Assenmacher from opposite sides and going down together in the dirt of the infield while the rest of the Indians piled on top.
