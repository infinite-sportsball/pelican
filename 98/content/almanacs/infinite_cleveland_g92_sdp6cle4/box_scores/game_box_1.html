San Diego 1998 Padres Are Champs
For six innings on this cold, rain-spattered October night, Cleveland's starter Bartolo Colon looked as though he had been put on earth solely to pitch this particular game — scattering four hits, loading the bases with nobody out in the sixth on a hit batsman and two walks and then escaping on a strikeout and two fly balls, his fastball gaining conviction even as his command wandered. The Indians had spotted him a 4-0 lead in a riotous third inning off the Padres' Kevin Brown, when five consecutive Cleveland batters reached base and Enrique Wilson's double into left-center scored two of them, sending the ballpark into a state of anticipatory delirium, the kind of noise a crowd makes when it can already taste the thing it has waited fifty years to swallow.

Then came the seventh, and the game turned inside out. Two singles off Colon and a walk by reliever Paul Shuey loaded the bases with two down, and San Diego's Ken Caminiti — who had fallen behind 0-2 and battled back through eight pitches — punched a groundball single through the right side that scored two and cut Cleveland's lead to 4-2. What followed was worse: Jim Leyritz, the Padres' designated hitter, drove a 1-2 pitch 389 feet into the right-center-field bleachers for a three-run homer that turned a four-run cushion into a one-run deficit, and out in the bleachers a kid in a Cleveland jersey — couldn't have been more than ten, eyes bright with fury — caught the ball on one hop and hurled it back onto the warning track while the Cleveland fans around him roared their approval, which was the loudest sound in a ballpark that had otherwise gone completely still. Tony Gwynn's run-scoring double off Jim Poole in the eighth made it 6-4, and by then it felt like punctuation on a sentence already written.

Randy Myers struck out the first two Cleveland batters in the bottom of the eighth, and then, inexplicably, walked three straight to load the bases — Lofton, Vizquel, Thome, the heart of the order, all aboard with two outs. The Padres' infielders stood on the wet grass looking at one another with the bewildered expressions of people who had just watched their closer lose the strike zone at the worst possible moment. But in the Cleveland dugout and in the stands above it, something electric was happening: the home crowd rising to their feet, the noise building from a low rumble to a roar that shook the rain off the upper-deck facades, because the bases were loaded and Manny Ramirez was coming to the plate and this, right here, was the moment the city had been waiting for — the tying run ninety feet away, the go-ahead run on second, the season refusing to die.

Then Bruce Bochy trotted to the mound and raised two fingers toward the bullpen, and the ballpark understood before the brain could process it. Trevor Hoffman. The roar didn't so much die as get swallowed whole — forty-five thousand throats closing at once, the stadium's electricity grounding out into the wet October earth. It was the cruelest paradox of this endless night: the very act of clawing back into striking distance was the thing that summoned the one man who made striking distance meaningless. Like a particle whose state collapses the moment you observe it, Cleveland's chances existed only so long as no one tested them — load the bases, force the issue, fight your way to the brink of something real, and the universe answers by sending Hoffman in from the bullpen to remind you that proximity to hope was never the same thing as hope itself. He needed exactly two pitches to end the eighth: a called strike and a fly ball to right that died softly in Gwynn's glove, three runners stranded where they stood. In the ninth he required four batters, working with the economy that comes to a closer who has spent an entire career refusing to give leads back, and when Jeff Branson's fly ball settled into Greg Vaughn's glove in left field, the Padres' catcher Carlos Hernandez was already sprinting toward the mound while Caminiti stood near third with both fists raised in the rain.

Across the diamond, the ballpark was already emptying — not in a rush, but in that slow, heavy way a stadium empties when the people inside it have nowhere they want to go. They filed down the concrete ramps of Jacobs Field and out into the October rain on Carnegie Avenue, and the ones who had driven sat in their cars in the garage for a long time with the radio still on, listening to the postgame show they didn't want to hear, because turning the key and driving home meant it was real. In the bars on Prospect and East Fourth the televisions stayed on but no one was watching anymore, just staring into pint glasses and not saying much, and in the houses on the east side and out past the river in Lakewood and Parma, people turned off their sets and sat in the silence of their living rooms and felt the particular weight of a loss that was not just tonight's but every night's — fifty years of almost, fifty years of reaching the last door and watching it close, and now this, the ninety-second and final closing, the one that said it was no longer a drought or a slump or a run of bad luck but simply the way things were and would always be. The rain fell on the empty parking lots of Jacobs Field and on the kid in the Cleveland jersey walking home with his old man, who had no words for him and would not have used them if he had, and on the whole gray sprawl of a city that had dared, for six innings, to believe that this time — this one last time — the story might end differently.
