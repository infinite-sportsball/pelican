San Diego 1998 Takes Title
Padres starter Kevin Brown had spent the evening making the baseball look small, working his sinker down into the cold dirt of Jacobs Field while forty-five thousand people in their October layers searched for reasons to believe. He gave up six hits and one earned run in seven and a third innings, and when he finally walked off in the eighth — Cleveland left fielder David Justice having fought a 2-2 count into an exhausting single through the left side — he left behind a lead as slender and as durable as a spider's thread: 2-1, San Diego, and closer Trevor Hoffman warming in the pen.

The game's single hinge swung in the fourth inning, when Cleveland catcher Einar Diaz drove a full-count line drive down the third-base line that skipped to the wall and brought third baseman Enrique Wilson home. The crowd rose with the sound that only a tying run in October can produce, a noise that begins in the gut and arrives at the throat a half-second later, and for three innings after that the game sat locked at one apiece, both starters grinding through zeroes as though each pitch were the last act of diplomacy before something irreversible. Indians right-hander Bartolo Colon, who matched Brown nearly frame for frame — seven innings, six hits, one earned run of his own — had already wriggled free of a bases-loaded, nobody-out jam in the fourth, when San Diego center fielder Steve Finley's grounder turned into a 6-2-3 double play and the air went briefly out of the Padres' rally like a tire losing pressure on a slow leak.

But in the seventh, with the sort of sequence that makes a season's worth of effort feel arbitrary, the thread snapped. First baseman Wally Joyner reached on Wilson's error, Finley survived a fielder's choice, shortstop Chris Gomez walked to load the bases, and Padres second baseman Quilvio Veras — the nine-hole hitter, the man nobody fears — worked the count full and lined a single into left that scored Finley and tilted the night toward San Diego for good. Cleveland loaded runners again in the bottom of the inning, Diaz and center fielder Kenny Lofton aboard with one out, but shortstop Omar Vizquel could only ground into a force, and first baseman Jim Thome flew out to center, and in this particular shuffling of the cards, Cleveland's curse endured. Hoffman needed just fourteen pitches for a 1-2-3 ninth, Cleveland's season ending on a weak fly ball off Lofton's bat that settled into Finley's glove like the period at the end of a long sentence.
