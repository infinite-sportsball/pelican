Cleveland 1998 Indians Are Champs
The ninth telling of this game was a conversation that neither team could end — a run answered by a run, a lead taken and immediately returned, the score knotted three separate times in the first five innings before Cleveland finally found a sentence that San Diego had no reply for. Omar Vizquel started it in the first, singling off Kevin Brown, stealing second without a throw, and scoring on Jim Thome's liner to left-center. Caminiti doubled and Finley singled him home to tie it in the second. Veras drove in Hernandez for a 2-1 Padres lead in the third. Enrique Wilson's two-out, two-run single through the left side in the fourth — Ramirez and Giles both scoring, the ball finding the one gap Brown had left unguarded — put Cleveland back ahead 3-2. Vaughn's sac fly in the fifth tied it again, and you could feel the game circling, as if it might go on trading blows all night in the forty-four-degree dark.

It didn't. In the bottom of the fifth, Kenny Lofton fouled off four pitches with two strikes before singling to right, then stole second on the next pitch. Vizquel, who seemed to find the gaps in this particular version of the evening the way water finds cracks, doubled to deep left on a 2-2 count to score Lofton and break the tie for the final time. When Vizquel himself came home on a Veras throwing error two batters later, the lead was 5-3 and something in the ballpark shifted — the sound changed, the air thickened, 45,225 people leaning forward in their seats as though they could push the remaining innings along by force of will.

Shuey and Assenmacher obliged, holding San Diego to a single hit over the final three frames. Assenmacher needed only eleven pitches to retire the ninth, the last out a Gwynn liner that Kenny Lofton ran down in right-center, and when he closed his glove around the final out the celebration that followed had the quality of something released — not just the end of a game but the breaking of a long and familiar silence, a city's worth of breath held since 1948 finally let go into the October night.
