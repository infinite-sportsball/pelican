Padres Take Title
For four and a half hours in the rain at Jacobs Field, in what may have been the most absurd shuffling of this particular deck, the two teams combined for thirty-five hits, twenty-two runs, nine pitchers, and an evening that seemed to belong to no one and everyone at once. The Padres scored four in the top of the first off Cleveland's Bartolo Colon — five consecutive singles plus two walks, the kind of inning where a starter's eyes go flat by the third batter — and by the time Greg Vaughn launched a solo homer into the left-field seats in the second to make it 5-1, the game looked settled. Colon lasted three innings, gave up eight runs on eight hits, and walked back to the dugout having thrown eighty-five pitches that accomplished almost nothing.

But Cleveland, as if the script demanded it, refused the blowout. Jim Thome tripled off the center-field wall in the third and scored on a David Justice sacrifice fly. In the fifth, trailing 10-4, Justice drove a 1-2 pitch from Kevin Brown three hundred and fifty-six feet into the right-field seats for a two-run homer, and when Einar Diaz singled home Enrique Wilson and Kenny Lofton ripped a triple into the right-center gap on a full count to score Diaz, it was 10-8 and the Padres had pulled Brown — their ace, their certainty — after only four innings and ninety-one pitches. By the seventh, Manny Ramirez singled to right off reliever Randy Myers to score Diaz and Lofton both, and at 11-10 the noise inside Jacobs Field was irrational, forty-five thousand people standing in the rain believing they had bent the evening back toward something like justice.

They had not bent it quite far enough. The Padres' catcher Carlos Hernandez lined an RBI double off Paul Shuey in the eighth to make it 12-10, and that was the margin that held. Thome doubled off Trevor Hoffman with one out in the ninth — the tying run at second, the go-ahead run in the on-deck circle, the stadium on its feet one last time — and the Padres pulled Hoffman for the left-hander Mark Langston, a move that conceded nothing except fear. Ramirez took a called third strike on the outside corner, frozen, the bat never leaving his shoulder. Justice lined the first pitch to right field, where it died in the glove of the fielder who was already jogging toward the mound before the ball arrived. Twelve to ten. Another Game 7 lost at Jacobs Field, another October night where Cleveland did everything but the last thing, where the comeback that shook the stadium for three innings ended with two men stranded in the cold and a visiting team celebrating on someone else's grass.
