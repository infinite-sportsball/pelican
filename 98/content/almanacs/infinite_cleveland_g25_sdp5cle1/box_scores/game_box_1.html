Padres Top Indians in World Series
In the twenty-fifth telling of this October night, the Padres' Tony Gwynn stepped in against Cleveland starter Bartolo Colon with Quilvio Veras on first and did not wait to see what Colon had in mind. He hit the first pitch 407 feet into the right-center seats — a two-run homer on a line drive that cleared the wall before the outfielders had finished turning around — and just like that it was 2-0 San Diego, the rain at Jacobs Field not yet heavy enough to blur the flight of the ball but cold enough, at forty-three degrees, to make 45,225 people pull their jackets tighter and wonder what kind of night this was going to be.

Cleveland answered in the bottom half — Kenny Lofton fought a 2-2 count into a ground-ball double down the left side, and Omar Vizquel singled him home on a liner through the shortstop hole — and for a few minutes the game felt like it might become something. Jim Thome beat out an infield single to put runners on first and second with one out, but Manny Ramirez grounded into a fielder's choice and David Justice struck out swinging, and that was the last time all night the Indians would have Kevin Brown in genuine trouble. Brown allowed eight hits in six and two-thirds innings and only that single run, his sinker boring into Cleveland bats all evening, twelve ground-ball outs piling up like sandbags against a flood that never came.

Colon, meanwhile, walked four Padres in five innings and threw a wild pitch, the kind of scattered, distracted start that lets a game slip away without any single catastrophic moment. San Diego added three in the fourth on a Steve Finley single to right that scored Ken Caminiti from third — Caminiti and Jim Leyritz having both walked — and a Carlos Hernandez single up the middle that scored Leyritz and Finley, the latter arriving home without a throw. At 5-1, the forty-three-degree rain falling harder now, the crowd at Jacobs Field began the terrible arithmetic that Cleveland crowds have been doing for half a century — counting outs, counting innings, counting the distance between what they came here to see and what they were being made to watch instead. In the sixth, David Justice walked, Brian Giles singled him to second, and Enrique Wilson singled to load the bases with one out — and then Jeff Branson grounded to second, Justice cut down at the plate, and Einar Diaz grounded to short to end it, the bases emptied without a run crossing the plate, 45,225 people exhaling into the cold rain with the particular resignation of a city that has learned, over fifty years, exactly what it feels like to have hope offered and then quietly taken back. Donne Wall retired the final seven Indians he faced, the last of them Lofton, who hit a 110-mile-per-hour grounder to short that Chris Gomez fielded cleanly and threw to first, and the ballpark went silent the way only a place that has been hoping for fifty years can go silent — not gradually, but all at once, as if someone had pulled the sound out of the building by the roots.
