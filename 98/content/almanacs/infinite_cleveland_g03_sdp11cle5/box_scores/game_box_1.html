San Diego 1998 Padres Win World Series
Ken Caminiti's solo home run in the second inning traveled only 339 feet, a line drive that barely cleared the right-field wall, but it carried with it the authority of a man who had decided early how this version of the evening would go. Greg Vaughn's two-run shot in the third — 380 feet into the left-field seats on a full count, after Gwynn had doubled and a wild pitch had moved him to third — made it 3-0 before Colon had found his footing, and from that point on the game took on the particular shape of a slow bleed: Cleveland scratching back a run here, a run there, never quite closing the wound.

David Justice doubled home Thome in the fourth to make it 3-1. Justice singled home Vizquel in the sixth to make it 3-2. In the seventh, with Wilson on third and Branson on second after a sacrifice and a wild pitch, Brown retired Lofton and Vizquel to strand them both, and 45,225 sat in the rain and the forty-degree cold and understood that the margin between still alive and already dead was exactly one hit that hadn't come. Then in the eighth, Brown finally left, and Manny Ramirez — who had struck out three times against the sinker — drove a 3-1 fastball from Randy Myers 378 feet into the left-field seats, a two-run homer that gave Cleveland a 5-4 lead and turned Jacobs Field into something that sounded less like a ballpark than a detonation.

It lasted four outs. Assenmacher entered for the ninth and the Padres simply dismantled him — Finley singled, Hernandez singled, Gomez walked, and Veras doubled two runs home to tie it before Assenmacher had recorded an out. Shuey inherited the wreckage and made it worse: Caminiti singled two more home, and then Jim Leyritz, on a 2-1 pitch, hit a three-run homer 405 feet to dead center, a ball that seemed to take the air out of the building on its way up. Seven runs in the inning. San Diego 11, Cleveland 5. Myers retired Cleveland in order in the ninth, and what had been, ten minutes earlier, the most electrifying moment of a fifty-year wait became instead the cruelest kind of October memory — the lead you held in your hands just long enough to believe it.
