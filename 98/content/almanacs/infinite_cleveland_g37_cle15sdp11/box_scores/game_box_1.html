On a night when the thermometer at Jacobs Field read forty-two degrees and the baseball kept leaving the yard as though the cold had offended it, Cleveland and San Diego combined for twenty-six runs, thirty-three hits, and five errors in a game that bore less resemblance to a World Series and more to a bar fight conducted with lumber. Both starters were shelled early — Bartolo Colon lasting just two and a third innings for the Indians, Kevin Brown lasting exactly three for San Diego, each man watching from the dugout as his bullpen tried to mop up a mess that wouldn't stop spreading.

The Padres' designated hitter, Jim Leyritz, took his first at-bat in the top of the second with Cleveland leading 1–0 and lined a single into right-center off Colon; a few pitches later Steve Finley's three-run homer had staked San Diego to a 3–1 lead, and Leyritz had been on base to see it start. Then Cleveland's second happened: five runs on five hits, a Kenny Lofton two-run double screaming into left-center at nearly a hundred and twelve miles an hour, a Jim Thome two-run double on a full-count pitch that sent Brown's catcher looking skyward as if for guidance. 6–3 Cleveland. In the top of the third Leyritz singled again — a line drive up the middle off Dave Burba this time — and stood on second, then third, while San Diego knotted it 6–6. The score kept moving like a car with a bad transmission. Cleveland scratched a run in the bottom of the third to lead 7–6. In the fifth, with the Indians clinging to that one-run lead, Leyritz saw a 0–1 pitch and lifted it three hundred and ninety-three feet into the left-center bleachers to tie it; two batters later San Diego had two more runs and a 9–7 advantage. Cleveland answered with two in the bottom of the fifth to tie it 9–9. And then in the top of the sixth, after Vaughn had put one three hundred and seventy feet into the right-field seats, Leyritz came up again and did it again — a 1–0 pitch driven three hundred and fifty-six feet down the left-field line, his second homer in as many innings, his fourth hit in four at-bats, and the Padres were back in front 11–9.

He would come up once more, in the eighth, with runners on and the score still 11–10, and Cleveland would decline to throw him a strike; the reliever worked around him for four straight balls, the loudest silence of the night at Jacobs Field. It was, in the arithmetic of the game, an admission — that Leyritz alone could not be pitched to any longer, that the only way San Diego was going to lose this game was for the rest of the lineup to fail to deliver behind him. Which, in the bottom of the seventh, was exactly what had already begun to happen.

The answer came in the seventh, against the left-hander Mark Langston. Omar Vizquel singled through the hole at short, Thome grounded one past third, and then Manny Ramirez — Cleveland's right fielder, the quiet center of every rally that mattered — turned on a first pitch and drove it to deep right-center for a double that tied the game at eleven. David Justice followed with a two-run double of his own on a 2–0 fastball, and before Langston could find his way off the mound, Jeff Branson had laced a two-run single into the gap to make it 15–11. Five runs, five hits, twenty-one pitches, and the arithmetic of the evening finally, irrevocably, tipped.

Paul Assenmacher needed just nine pitches to get through the ninth, and when Tony Gwynn lifted a fly ball to center to end it, the noise that rose from forty-five thousand throats carried in it fifty years of waiting — all of it breaking open at once in the cold Cleveland dark.
