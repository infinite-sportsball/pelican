Indians KO Padres
For seven innings at Jacobs Field, the Padres' Kevin Brown and Cleveland's Bartolo Colon pitched as if the October night itself had narrowed to the width of a strike zone — Brown with eleven strikeouts in seven innings, the sinker snapping down through bat after futile bat, and Colon with eight of his own in six, working 111 pitches through a lineup he kept off-balance with a fastball that seemed to arrive angrier each time. The game's only run came in the sixth, when Colon walked Wally Joyner with the bases loaded and two outs, a pitch that missed by inches and scored Greg Vaughn from third to give San Diego a 1–0 lead that felt, given how Brown was pitching, like it might be enough.

It was not enough, and the way it ended will haunt Randy Myers for as long as he cares to remember. Brown left after seven innings and 116 pitches, having allowed six hits and one earned run in a performance that deserved a better fate, and Myers inherited a one-run lead in the bottom of the eighth. Manny Ramirez fought back from 0–2 to single. David Justice worked a 2–2 count and lined one through the middle. Then Cleveland's manager sent Travis Fryman up to pinch-hit for Enrique Wilson, and Fryman swung at the first pitch he saw and drove it 437 feet into left-center, a three-run homer that turned a 1–0 deficit into a 3–1 lead and turned forty-five thousand people into something that sounded like a single, sustained, disbelieving voice.

Myers was not done being punished. Einar Diaz walked, Kenny Lofton singled, a wild pitch scored Diaz from third, and then Jim Thome — who had struck out twice against Brown and popped up weakly in the seventh — pulled a 1–1 pitch 460 feet into the right-field seats for a three-run homer that made the score 7–1 and emptied what was left of the competitive air from the building. Seven runs in the eighth, all of them off Myers, and when Carlos Hernandez grounded out to end the ninth against Jim Poole, the roar that came out of Jacobs Field was the kind of noise a city makes when fifty years of waiting finally breaks apart and washes away.
