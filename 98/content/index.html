Each day, Sunday, October 25, 1998, the players wake up, start their day, prepare for their game, and take the field at Jacobs Field on a cold October night for the seventh and final game of the World Series. Each night, the simulator generates a record of what occurred during that timeline. Each day, the players wake up, and once again it is Sunday, October 25, 1998.

 

1998 World Series Game 7

By 1998, the Cleveland Indians had spent the better part of a decade building one of the most fearsome lineups in baseball. Thome, Ramirez, Lofton, Justice, Vizquel — on paper, they could hit with anyone. And every October, they proved it. They just never proved it enough. The Braves took the World Series from them in 1995. The Marlins ripped it away in a Game 7 for the ages in 1997. The Yankees shoved them aside in the '98 ALCS, three straight after Cleveland had taken the lead. By October of 1998, the question had stopped being whether Cleveland was talented enough. The question was whether October would ever let them in.

In this timeline, Cleveland finally breaks through — and draws the San Diego Padres. San Diego is a franchise in crisis, a city where the future of baseball is genuinely in doubt, and yet somehow they've put together a team that has no business being here and keeps winning anyway. Tony Gwynn and Greg Vaughn can hit, but what makes San Diego dangerous is what happens on the mound. Kevin Brown pitches like he's trying to hurt you — a 2.38 ERA built on aggression and nasty, late-breaking stuff that eats through lineups. And when Brown is done, the ninth inning belongs to Trevor Hoffman. Fifty-three saves. Hells Bells ringing through the stadium. Ballgame over.

Cleveland sends Bartolo Colón to the mound for Game 7. Colón is electric and unpredictable — capable of brilliance or combustion, sometimes in the same inning. On his best night, he can go toe-to-toe with anyone. On his worst, the game is over by the fourth. That volatility is what makes this matchup worth watching a hundred times over: Brown is a machine, and machines are consistent, but Colón is a live wire, and a live wire in a Game 7 can change everything — or short-circuit entirely.

 

Dipping into the Pool of Infinite Timelines

What if Colón finds that extra gear and deals seven shutout innings? What if Thome catches one in the fifth and the crowd at Jacobs Field blows the roof off? What if Cleveland builds a lead early and never has to see Hoffman at all? Or what if Brown is untouchable, carving through the Cleveland lineup like he's done all year, and Hoffman jogs in from the bullpen to a stadium that still remembers what happened in the ninth inning of the last Game 7?

Imagine the infinite timelines that flow through Game 7. Each timeline begins with the same batters and the same pitchers in the same lineup order; but each timeline holds the promise of a different ending to Cleveland's long October. Some of those endings are the ones this city has been waiting for since 1948. Some of them are the same heartbreak Cleveland knows too well. And some of them go places nobody could have predicted.

To dip into the pool of infinite timelines for Cleveland, we simulated Game 7 of the 1998 World Series using Out of the Park Baseball Simulator. Each simulation used the real Game 7 lineups and starting positions for each team. Each team had the real Game 7 starting pitchers on the mound in each simulation.
