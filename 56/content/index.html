Each day, Monday, October 8, 1956, Don Larsen wakes up bleary-eyed and slightly hungover, wondering if manager Casey Stengel will pass him over for Game 5 after his lousy showing in Game 2. The thought of a perfect game never crosses his mind, and never will. Each day, somewhere deep in the bowels of Yankee Stadium, Stengel opens a locker and places a baseball inside Larsen’s shoe, dooming him to the mound. Each afternoon, the simulator manufactures a new, pristine Brooklyn win, and Larsen hits the showers, defeated. Each day, Larsen wakes up, and once again it is Monday, October 8, 1956.

 

1956 World Series Game 5

In the 1956 World Series, the National League champion Brooklyn Dodgers faced off with the American League champion New York Yankees. It was a rematch of the 1955 Fall Classic, where Brooklyn had finally beaten the Yankees to claim their first championship. The Dodgers, managed by Walter Alston, featured a lineup loaded with future Hall of Famers: Duke Snider, Jackie Robinson, Roy Campanella, and Pee Wee Reese. The Yankees, managed by Casey Stengel, countered with Mickey Mantle, Yogi Berra, and a deep pitching staff.

With the series tied 2-2, Stengel gave the Game 5 start to Don Larsen, a journeyman right-hander who had been knocked out early in Game 2. What followed was the most improbable pitching performance in baseball history: Larsen retired all 27 Dodgers batters he faced, throwing the only perfect game in World Series history. Mantle provided the offense with a home run, and the Yankees won 2-0. The Dodgers never got a baserunner. No walks, no errors, no hit batsmen. 97 pitches. Perfection.

Dipping into the Pool of Infinite Timelines

Don Larsen's perfect game is the most singular achievement in World Series history. It was as unlikely as it was humiliating, but it was also a game pregnant with opportunity for the Dodgers. What if Gil Hodges had pulled that line drive a few inches to the right? What if Jackie Robinson's second-inning liner had been a foot higher? What if the normally stone-cold patient Dodgers hadn't been so uncharacteristically aggressive at the plate? One pitch, one bounce, one call could have unraveled the whole thing, gotten into Larsen's head, and turned a legendary afternoon into an early trip to the showers.

Imagine the infinite timelines that flow through Game 5. Each timeline begins with the same batters and the same pitchers in the same lineup order; but in this universe, each timeline holds the promise of a brand new disaster for Don Larsen and a brand new World Series ring for the Dodgers.

To dip into the pool of infinite timelines for "Don Larsen is a Bum", we simulated Game 5 of the 1956 World Series using Out of the Park Baseball Simulator. Each simulation used the real Game 5 lineups and starting positions for each team. Each team had the real Game 5 starting pitchers on the mound in each simulation.
