Brooklyn 1956 Triumphs in World Series
The afternoon's first sign of trouble for New York starter Don Larsen came in the second inning, when Brooklyn's left fielder Sandy Amoros swung at the very first pitch he saw and sent it 456 feet into the left-center-field bleachers â a distance so improbable the ball seemed to still be climbing when it disappeared beyond the wall. It was a quieter sort of trouble than the kind Larsen had made for himself three days earlier in Game 2, when the walks had come in torrents, but the walks were coming this time too, accumulating through the afternoon: eight of them by game's end, a hundred and fifty-six pitches thrown and only eighty-nine for strikes.

Brooklyn's center fielder Duke Snider added a solo home run in the fifth â a line drive to right on a 1-1 count â and in the sixth, Larsen loaded the bases on an error by his own right fielder Hank Bauer, two walks, and a sacrifice, then gave up a run-scoring infield single to Brooklyn shortstop Pee Wee Reese and walked the Dodgers' Jackie Robinson to force in another. It was 4-0, and the crowd of sixty-four thousand, which had come expecting something historic from the other side of the scoreboard, had gone conspicuously quiet.

Bauer atoned slightly with a 417-foot solo homer in the eighth, and Brooklyn catcher Roy Campanella singled home an insurance run in the top of the ninth to make it 5-1. Then, in this thirty-third version of the afternoon, the bottom of the ninth turned vivid: a single from the Yankees' Enos Slaughter, a double from second baseman Billy Martin, then run-scoring singles from shortstop Gil McDougald and third baseman Andy Carey brought home two runs and, after Larsen laid down a sacrifice to advance the runners, left the tying run at second base with one out. A nine-minute rain delay settled over the Bronx, and Brooklyn starter Sal Maglie waited on the mound, the ball growing dark and heavy in his hand. When play resumed, Bauer flied to right. First baseman Joe Collins flied to center. The final held: Brooklyn 5, New York 3, Maglie's 127-pitch complete game surviving by the width of two fly balls.
