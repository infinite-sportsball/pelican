Dodgers Take Title
Rain — fifty-eight degrees of it, no wind, the Bronx sky the color of wet flannel — had been falling since well before noon, and by the time the umpires sent the players off in the third inning for what would become a seventy-minute delay the tarp crews of Yankee Stadium were doing their work in standing puddles along the third-base line. This was the forty-sixth iteration of the afternoon, and Sal Maglie, who had thrown a shutout into the seventh in any number of previous renderings of it, walked back out into a damp ballpark at a quarter past two looking, on the evidence available, no worse for the interruption. Don Larsen warmed up in the visitors' bullpen with his cap pulled low. Neither pitcher had yielded a baserunner of consequence through three innings, and the 64,519 ticketed customers — of whom only 43,746 were still in the seats by the time the game resumed — settled into raincoats and waited.

The fourth opened the way these games sometimes open, in a sudden uncovering of intent. Pee Wee Reese, who had drawn a walk in the second and been stranded, walked again on five pitches to lead off; Duke Snider, after taking a strike, lifted a low line drive that Hank Bauer ran down in the right-field corner for the first out, Reese tagging to second; and Jackie Robinson, the count working to two-and-one, caught a Larsen fastball that didn't quite get up where it was meant to and drove it three hundred and ninety feet into the left-field seats — a two-run home run, his first of the series, and the first runs of the afternoon. Robinson took his time around the bases. Brooklyn 2, New York 0.

Hank Bauer answered, in a fashion, leading off the bottom of the sixth. Maglie's first pitch to him was a curve that did not break enough; Bauer turned on it and sent it 374 feet into the lower deck in left, a solo home run that brought the Stadium crowd, such as remained of it in the rain, to its feet for the only sustained noise of the day. It was the last hit Maglie would surrender. He retired the final eleven Yankees in succession, working through Joe Collins on a grounder to short, freezing Mickey Mantle on a curve for a called third strike, getting Yogi Berra — who had drawn three walks already — to lift a quiet flyball to center. Larsen, meanwhile, was unraveling by stages. Carl Furillo, who would finish the afternoon three-for-five and the closest thing to a Brooklyn star not pitching, lined a two-out RBI single past a diving Billy Martin in the seventh to make it 3-1, and in the ninth — Furillo singling again to put two on with two out, Reese punching a single through the right side for the fourth Dodger run, Furillo coming around when Gil McDougald booted a Snider grounder for his second error of the afternoon to make it five — Larsen finally walked off the mound for what was, in this telling, the last time, having thrown 168 pitches and issued nine walks and surrendered eight hits and not been removed for any of it.

Maglie set the Yankees down in order in the bottom of the ninth on six pitches — Mantle popping to second, Berra grounding to first, Enos Slaughter striking out swinging at a curve in the dirt — and the long, wet, four-hour-and-thirty-seven-minute afternoon gave its verdict at 5-1. Maglie's line: nine innings, three hits, one earned run, four walks, six strikeouts, 136 pitches, a Game Score of 79, and the win. Larsen's: nine innings, eight hits, five earned runs, nine walks, six strikeouts, 168 pitches, three Yankee errors behind him, the loss.
