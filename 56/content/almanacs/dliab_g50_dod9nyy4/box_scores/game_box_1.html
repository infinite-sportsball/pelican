Brooklyn 1956 Tops New York 1956
The afternoon announced its intentions early and often, though not always to the same team. Brooklyn scored twice in the top of the first on a Duke Snider single through the drawn-in infield that sent Jim Gilliam racing home from third, and then a two-out Sandy Amoros liner to right-center that brought Reese around from second, and for a few minutes it seemed as though Larsen might not survive the opening act. But Mickey Mantle, batting with Joe Collins on first and two down in the bottom half, took a 1-2 pitch from Sal Maglie and deposited it 414 feet into the right-field seats, a swing so sudden and complete that the sound preceded the understanding — the crack first, then the collective recognition of sixty-four thousand people that the game was tied. When Larsen himself singled home Billy Martin in the second to give New York a 3-2 lead, the stadium had the warm, self-satisfied hum of a crowd that believes it is watching destiny reassemble itself. Larsen, though, could not stop nibbling at the edges. He walked three consecutive Dodgers in the third — Hodges, Amoros after a grueling nine-pitch at-bat, and Furillo on four straight balls — loading the bases without allowing a run only because Campanella grounded into a force and Maglie grounded to short. The escape looked like resilience at the time. In retrospect it looked like a man who had already lost the strike zone and was borrowing innings he could not afford. And in the fourth, with two out and the bases again full of Dodgers who had scratched their way on through sheer persistence — Reese lining a single, Robinson beating out a slow grounder, Hodges poking another through the infield on the first pitch he saw — Amoros took a 1-2 pitch and drove it on a line to deep left, the ball carrying over Enos Slaughter's head and rolling to the wall while three Brooklyn runners came home in succession, each touching the plate with the unhurried certainty of men who had done this before and would, perhaps, do it again. The bases-clearing triple turned a 3-2 deficit into a 5-3 Brooklyn lead, and in the five quiet innings that followed, with Maglie setting down Yankee after Yankee on soft grounders and harmless popups — retiring twelve of the next thirteen men he faced — the old ballpark seemed to recognize the shape of the afternoon: another October Monday slipping away from the home team, the way they always seemed to now.

The debt came due in the ninth. Robinson, leading off, took two balls and then lashed a triple to deep right-center, the ball splitting the gap and bounding to the wall, and he stood on third base the way Robinson always stood on third base — leaning toward home, daring someone to do something about it. Larsen, who had already thrown 148 pitches, could have pitched around the situation but no longer had the command to pitch around anything. He walked Hodges on a full count. He walked Amoros on four of five pitches to load the bases. And then Carl Furillo — Brooklyn's right fielder, who had gone hitless all afternoon, who had walked twice and flied out and done nothing to announce himself — stepped in and worked the count full, fouling off the first two pitches, taking three balls, the at-bat stretching out like a held breath in the upper deck. On the seventh pitch, Larsen threw a fastball that did not get far enough inside, and Furillo swung and connected and the ball rose on a line toward left field, still climbing as it passed over Slaughter, still climbing as it cleared the wall, 423 feet into an October sky that had gone from partly cloudy to fully decided. A grand slam. Four runs scoring. The silence that followed was not the silence of a stunned crowd calculating what had just gone wrong — it was something rarer, the hush of sixty-four thousand people watching a baseball leave a ballpark and recognizing, despite themselves, that they had just seen something magnificent, a swing so pure and an arc so inevitable that partisanship fell away for a half-second and there was only the ball against the sky and the deepening roar from the Brooklyn dugout and the small figure of Furillo rounding the bases with his head down, as though he had not just broken the game open but merely completed an errand he'd been meaning to get to all afternoon. The score was 9-3. Larsen walked off having thrown 163 pitches to 43 batters, which is less a pitching line than an endurance test, and the look on his face as he handed the ball to Bob Cerv and descended into the dugout was the look of a man who has woken from the same bad dream too many times to be surprised by it anymore.

Maglie went the distance for Brooklyn on 144 pitches, scattering seven hits and striking out seven, bending but never quite breaking — he walked four Yankees himself, hit Bauer, allowed Mantle's home run, and still managed to strand eight New York runners over the course of the afternoon, the work of a craftsman who understood that in October you do not need to be dominant, only sufficient. In the bottom of the ninth, with the crowd already thinning and the shadows long across the infield, he allowed a run on a Carey single and loaded the bases on a walk to Cerv, and for a brief flickering moment the old stadium stirred as though the afternoon might yet have something left to say. But Maglie struck out Bauer looking, got Collins on a lazy popup to Robinson at third, and then — with McDougald on third and the tying run at the plate — froze Mantle on a called third strike at the knees, the kind of pitch that leaves a hitter standing in the box with his bat on his shoulder and nothing to say, sixty-four thousand witnesses already turning for the exits before the umpire's hand came up. The final score was 9-4, Brooklyn, and whatever the afternoon had once promised the home team, it had kept none of it.
