Brooklyn 1956 Wins World Series Crown
Twenty-five runs were scored at Yankee Stadium on this particular reshuffling of the October afternoon, and the odd thing is that for five innings it looked like it might be a game. Larsen and Maglie traded zeroes through two, Brooklyn scratched out four in the third on a string of infield hits and sacrifice flies and aggressive baserunning that suggested a team playing with the casual ferocity of men who already know how this ends, and the Yankees answered with two of their own — Mantle doubling to right-center, Berra singling him home — and then Collins tripled in the fifth and scored to make it 4-3. The line on the scoreboard was still plausible. And then the sixth inning happened.

Robinson walked, Hodges doubled him to third, and Furillo lined a single to center at 113 miles an hour that scored both of them and restored the air of inevitability that had briefly left the building. Two outs later, with Furillo still on first, Sal Maglie — thirty-nine years old, a right-handed pitcher with no business doing what he was about to do — turned on an 0-1 pitch and drove it 477 feet into the right-center-field bleachers. Four hundred and seventy-seven feet. From the pitcher. Larsen stood on the mound and watched the ball leave and kept watching long after it had landed, and then he was gone, replaced by Bob Cerv, who would fare no better. The game had ceased to be a contest and become a kind of arithmetic, the Dodgers adding runs in every subsequent inning like a clerk tallying receipts.

Hodges hit two more home runs — a two-run shot off Cerv in the seventh that traveled 475 feet, and a three-run blast off Howard in the eighth — and Gilliam added a pair of his own, and by the time Maglie finally handed the ball to Rube Walker with two outs in the ninth the score was 18-7 and the Yankees had used every arm they had. Maglie himself had gone 3-for-4 at the plate with 129 pitches on the mound, which is the sort of line that belongs to a man for whom the distinction between pitcher and position player has become, for one warm afternoon in the Bronx, temporarily irrelevant.
