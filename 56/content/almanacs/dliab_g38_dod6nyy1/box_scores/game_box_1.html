Jim Gilliam, Brooklyn's second baseman, hit the second pitch of the game into the right-center gap at Yankee Stadium and was standing on third base before Don Larsen had properly begun his afternoon. What followed â a Snider grounder that went through Billy Martin's legs at second, a Gil Hodges single, and then Sandy Amoros's two-out triple to deep right-center that emptied the bases â took perhaps ten minutes, and by the end of it the score was 3-0 and Larsen had the look of a man who has opened the wrong door. He would throw 175 pitches before the day was through, all nine innings, as though the Yankee bench had decided that if he was going to lose he might as well lose completely, using up every arm's length of himself in the process.

The fourth inning put it away. Roy Campanella, Brooklyn's catcher, drove a solo home run 444 feet to left on a 2-1 pitch, a ball that was still climbing when it crossed the wall and landed somewhere in the bleachers where October crowds sit with their coat collars turned up. Maglie walked, Gilliam walked, and Reese singled to load the bases; a Snider sacrifice fly and a Jackie Robinson double to right-center brought in two more and made it 6-0. Larsen walked seven men on the day and struck out four, and the arithmetic there tells you everything â he spent the entire afternoon trying to find the strike zone and mostly failing, throwing 175 pitches of which barely more than half were strikes.

Maglie, for his part, tossed a three-hitter. He struck out seven and walked one and needed only 108 pitches to get through all nine innings, the game's only blemish a Yogi Berra solo homer in the seventh that cleared the right-field wall by the narrowest of margins, 338 feet of grudging distance, and put New York on the board at 6-1. It was the kind of run that exists only to keep the shutout column honest. In the ninth he struck out Mantle looking on a 1-2 pitch and got Berra on a lazy fly to left, the crowd slowly dispersed into the cold evening, every last Yankee fan fighting the intense discomfort of having witnessed humiliation at the hands of their most bitter rivals.
