Dodgers Win 1956 World Series
For six innings, sixty-four thousand believers in pinstriped inevitability had every reason to lean back in their seats and enjoy the afternoon. Don Larsen had not allowed a hit. Billy Martin's solo home run in the second and a two-run third — Collins and Mantle singling, Berra driving one through to right-center, Slaughter doubling into the left-field corner to score two — had given New York a 3-0 lead that felt carved in granite, the kind of cushion a Yankee team accepts as its natural inheritance. Brooklyn's hitters looked overmatched, bewildered, swinging through fastballs or watching them clip corners. Larsen had thrown sixty pitches through six innings, and the only blemish on his line was a few walks that had come to nothing. The old stadium smelled like cigar smoke and coronation.

Then the seventh inning opened like a wound that would not close. Jackie Robinson walked. Gil Hodges, down 0-2, got a pitch he could lift and drove it 429 feet into the left-field seats — a two-run homer that sheared two runs off the lead in a single swing and sent a tremor through the grandstand that you could feel in the concrete. Larsen, who had looked untouchable, suddenly could not find the strike zone with a surveyor's map: he walked Amoros, walked Campanella, threw twenty-eight pitches in the inning and got only half of them over the plate. With two outs and the bases full, Jim Gilliam — hitless all day — lashed a line drive to right-center at 115 miles an hour, a two-run double that emptied the bases and flipped the scoreboard to Brooklyn 4, New York 3. The Dodgers, who had spent six innings doing absolutely nothing, had scored four runs on two hits and suddenly owned the game. Every Yankee in the dugout knew what that silence in the grandstand meant.

They would hear more of it. Snider crushed a full-count pitch 411 feet to open the eighth. Amoros hit a two-run shot off Larsen that finally ended him — pulled after seven and a third innings, eight walks, seventy strikes in a hundred and twenty-eight pitches, a man drowning one free pass at a time. Cerv came in and fared worse: four walks and an RBI single by Gilliam in the eighth, then three home runs in the ninth — Hodges again, 415 feet, his second of the day; Campanella, 410 feet; and then Maglie himself, Brooklyn's pitcher, driving a first-pitch fastball 456 feet to right-center, a shot so gratuitous it amounted to humiliation. The Dodgers scored thirteen runs in the final three innings of a game in which they had not managed a single hit through six, and the Yankees, who had led 3-0 and looked like the team that always wins in October, absorbed the kind of defeat that leaves a mark on the franchise's memory — the kind Brooklyn had been dreaming of inflicting since the day they built Ebbets Field.
