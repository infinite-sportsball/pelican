Dodgers Win 1956 World Series
On an afternoon when the October light seemed almost too generous for what was about to unfold, Brooklyn's Sal Maglie took the mound at Yankee Stadium and pitched as though the sixty-four thousand in attendance were irrelevant to the conversation he was having with the strike zone — nine innings, three hits, not a single run crossing the plate. The old Barber, as they used to call him, needed only a hundred and fifteen pitches to shave the entire Yankee lineup down to stubble, and if his control wandered now and then — four walks — the zeros kept accumulating on the scoreboard like an argument no one could refute.

The Dodgers, meanwhile, treated Don Larsen the way a river treats a sand levee. It began modestly enough in the second, when Maglie himself — the pitcher, batting ninth — lashed a double to right-center that scored Sandy Amoros and Roy Campanella, both of whom had reached ahead of him. Amoros added an RBI double in the third, and for a few innings the 3-0 lead sat there quietly, the game feeling decided without quite being emphatic. Then came the seventh. Pee Wee Reese doubled down the left-field line, Larsen walked Snider intentionally and Robinson singled to load the bases, and after Hodges struck out on a checked swing that made you wince, Amoros drew a walk that forced in Reese. Carl Furillo then drove the second pitch he saw to deep left-center field — four hundred and eighty-two feet into seats where no one had thought to worry about foul balls — a grand slam that turned a shutout into an annihilation. Larsen was gone three batters later, having walked six and allowed eight runs, the sort of line that makes you wonder whether the man even recognized where he was.

Brooklyn piled on three more in the ninth, including a two-run home run from Reese and a solo shot from Duke Snider, the final score reading 13-0 like a typographical error. In the bottom of the ninth, Maglie loaded the bases on two singles and a walk — the closest New York came all afternoon to something resembling hope — before Enos Slaughter grounded into a 4-6-3 double play that ended it. Thirteen to nothing. In the history that was supposed to happen here, Larsen would throw twenty-seven men down in order and walk off the field into immortality. Instead he walked six Dodgers, gave up eight runs, and was sitting in the dugout by the seventh inning while his replacement gave up four more. Some afternoons the universe doesn't just correct the story — it humiliates the author.
