World Series to Dodgers
The Yankees came out as though intending to settle the matter early — Joe Collins's walk, Mickey Mantle catching a 2-2 Maglie fastball and sending it into the deep right-center grass for an inside-the-park home run, Andy Carey's two-out double driving home two more — and by the time the side was retired the Stadium scoreboard read 5-0 and sixty-four thousand New Yorkers were settling in for what looked, in this particular reshuffling of the afternoon, like a coronation.

Brooklyn's answer arrived in pieces rather than a wave. Carl Furillo, the Dodgers' right fielder, pulled a 2-2 Larsen fastball down the left-field line in the second for a solo home run, and two innings later he was at it again — singling, going to third on Sal Maglie's line single, and crossing the plate when Jim Gilliam tapped a sixty-five-mile-an-hour grounder back to the mound that Larsen lunged for and threw away, the kind of soft chopper pitchers field a thousand times in March and forget how to handle in October. Yogi Berra's solo shot off Maglie in the seventh made it 6-2, Sandy Amoros's two-run drive into the right-field seats in the eighth pulled it to 6-4, and Larsen — who had by then struck out eleven Dodgers, working through an afternoon in which he was almost dominant and never quite in control — came back out for the ninth, his pitch count climbing past 160.

Larsen had thrown one hundred and sixty-two pitches by the time Jim Gilliam stepped in to lead off the ninth and doubled down the right-field line. Pee Wee Reese took four pitches, none of them within a foot of the strike zone. Duke Snider lined a single up the middle to load the bases with no one out, and now Jackie Robinson stepped in with the count working full and the lead pared to a single run. Larsen missed with a fastball that wasn't close, and Robinson trotted to first as Gilliam walked home with the run that made it 6-5. The first boos came down out of the upper deck then — scattered, almost embarrassed, the sound Yankee crowds make when they don't yet want to believe what they are watching. Casey Stengel did not move from the dugout. Bob Cerv stayed sitting in the bullpen. Larsen, who had stood on this same mound three days earlier in Game 2 and walked four Brooklyn batters in a single inning to give back a six-run lead, now faced Gil Hodges with the bases still loaded and no one yet out. Ball one. Ball two, low. Strike, called. Ball three. The boos thickened with each delivery, gathered themselves into something that was no longer scattered, and on 3-1 Larsen threw his seventh walk of the afternoon and Pee Wee Reese walked home with the run that tied it, and the Stadium came down around him — sixty-four thousand voices in the unmistakable Bronx register, the one that calls a man a bum to his face and means it. Sandy Amoros grounded into a force at the plate for the first out. Carl Furillo, the Dodgers' right fielder and by now the Player of the Game in everything but the formal notation, lined Larsen's 177th pitch into shallow right; Jackie Robinson scored from third, Gil Hodges came around on a wave from his coach and beat the throw home, and Brooklyn led 8-6.

Now, finally, Stengel came. He climbed the dugout steps slowly, the old man's gait that conceded nothing, and made the long walk to the mound with one finger raised toward the bullpen and Bob Cerv already trotting in from right-center. Larsen stood waiting, glove up over the lower half of his face, eyes fixed somewhere past Stengel's left shoulder, and the boos — which had been raining down on him since the Hodges walk — found a second target now and rained down on his manager too, the particular fury Yankee crowds reserve for the slow hook that came an inning and three walks too late. Stengel took the ball without a word. Larsen walked off the mound looking at no one. Cerv struck out Roy Campanella, hit the pinch-hitting Ransom Jackson, and got Gilliam to ground out to second to end the half, and the booing did not stop until the Dodgers had taken the field. Randy Jackson, who had relieved Maglie in the eighth, set the Yankees down in the bottom half — Joe Collins grounded to first, Mantle lifted a quiet fly to center, Berra walked, Enos Slaughter popped to short — and the long October afternoon, four hours and one minute by the Stadium clock, gave its verdict at 8-6 and emptied out into the upper Bronx, Larsen's final line reading 8.1 innings, ten hits, eight runs, seven walks, eleven strikeouts, one hundred and seventy-seven pitches, and one defeat.
